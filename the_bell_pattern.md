# The Bell Pattern: Where the Time-Feel Comes From

*Part 2 of a series on rhythm in Black American music. Part 1 ("Counting the Uncountable") covered jazz and hip-hop time signatures, and ended on a promise: to go further back — into the African polyrhythmic origins that the Cuban inheritance carried into jazz, and the ways Western notation fails to capture what is actually happening on the page. This is that essay.*

---

## A note on what this essay is trying to do

Part 1 closed with a diagnostic. When music sounds rhythmically "weird" to a listener trained on European common-practice meter, it is almost always (a) actual odd meter — 5/4, 7/8; (b) 4/4 with a *cross-rhythm* riding on top — two pulses coexisting; or (c) 4/4 with *micro-timing displacement* — the grid is intact, but events sit ahead of or behind where the grid says they should.

Most of Part 2 is the origin story of category (b). The cross-rhythm tradition does not come from "syncopation" — a Western frame that presumes a privileged downbeat being deviated from. It comes from a different organizational logic, in which two or more pulses are co-equal and the music's identity is the *interaction between them*. The logic arrived in the Americas in two flows — one through Cuba, one direct into the Anglophone South — and those flows met in New Orleans before traveling outward into jazz, blues, gospel, funk, and everything downstream. The inheritance also shapes category (c): Dilla's displacement, Zigaboo Modeliste's elastic snare — the cultivated ability to sit *off* the grid without losing it — is the same temperament at a finer resolution.

One framing move before any of this. Because much of what follows touches a tradition the writer is not from, the carefulness has to be enacted, not declared. The patterns are specific to specific peoples and pieces — Ewe Agbadza, Yoruba bembé, Mandé Dundunba, Shona Nhemamusasa — and they are no more "African rhythm" than a Bach fugue is "European harmony." Specificity is the only courtesy the music is owed.

## The Agawu correction

The Ghanaian-born musicologist Kofi Agawu, in *Representing African Music* (Routledge, 2003) and the earlier essay "The Invention of 'African Rhythm'" (*JAMS* 48/3, 1995), makes an argument that has to be on the table before this essay can proceed in good faith. Agawu's claim: the *concept* "African rhythm" — the notion that African music is distinctively or essentially rhythmic, in opposition to a "harmonic-melodic" Western tradition — is a colonial construction. It is not that polyrhythm does not exist in African music. It plainly does. The trouble is the framing that treats rhythm as the *master-key feature* distinguishing African from European practice. European music is also polyrhythmic — Renaissance polyphony is full of cross-rhythm, Beethoven scherzos run hemiola against the bar, mensural notation is built on simultaneous duple-and-triple readings — but the framing is rarely applied. The asymmetry is the colonial residue.

Agawu's correction is not "polyrhythm is a myth." It is "polyrhythm is a feature, not a category." The honest move is to name what the music does, in pieces, with people, in places, rather than to project a single attribute onto a continent. When this essay says "the standard pattern," it means a specific seven-stroke key pattern across twelve pulses, played on a forged double iron bell called a *gankogui* in Ewe Agbadza performance. When it says "clave," it means a sixteen-pulse organizational axis with a specific internal grouping that came out of Cuba.

## The standard pattern, slowly

If there is one rhythmic figure that anchors the largest share of West and Central African ensemble repertoires, and that crossed the Atlantic intact, it is this one. Seven attacks distributed across twelve pulses, with inter-onset intervals of **2-2-1-2-2-2-1**. Strokes on pulses **1, 3, 5, 6, 8, 10, 12**.

Twelve pulses can be heard as four groups of three — compound duple, what Western notation calls 12/8. The same twelve can be heard as six groups of two — simple triple. Or three groups of four. The pattern is asymmetric on every reading. It does not divide evenly into the four-group hearing or the six-group hearing. That asymmetry is the point.

Master Anlo-Ewe drummer C.K. Ladzekpo, in his "Foundation Course in African Dance-Drumming" (ladzekpo.com), frames cross-rhythm as the *generating principle* of these musics — not ornamentation laid on top of a meter, but the structural fact from which the music is built. Cultivated players hold the duple and triple readings simultaneously. David Locke, in "Yewevu in the Metric Matrix" (*Music Theory Online* 16/4, 2010), proposes a "metric matrix" model that retains both readings at once rather than forcing the analyst to pick — a strategy that respects what the players are actually doing.

A.M. Jones, in *Studies in African Music* (Oxford, 1959), gave Western analysis its first ambitious treatment. The label "standard pattern" was introduced by Jones's Ewe informant Desmond Tay; Jones recorded and propagated it. He got the importance of the polyrhythm right; he placed bar lines in ways later work by Agawu and Locke has shown to misrepresent where the players' own cycles begin. Notation is downstream of practice. When they disagree, the practice wins.

The pattern is the timeline of Ewe Agbadza, Atsiagbekor, Adzogbo. It is the bell pattern of Yoruba bembé. It is the structural reference of Cuban iyesá and Cuban 6/8 bembé. The duple-pulse version of the same logic — sixteen pulses instead of twelve — is the *clave*.

J.H. Kwabena Nketia's term for the role this pattern plays — *time-line* — is the cleanest single word for what is happening. The bell does not provide a beat. It provides an externalized, fixed, asymmetric reference against which everything else can be played. Eugene Novotney's dissertation (*The Three Against Two Relationship as the Foundation of Timelines in West African Musics*, Univ. of Illinois, 1998) argues that 3:2 hemiola is the foundational interval out of which the standard pattern and most of its relatives are built. The standard pattern is one elaboration. The clave is another.

### The bell is not "syncopated"

This is the place to retire the word *syncopation*. Syncopation, in the European sense, is a deviation from a privileged downbeat — an accent placed where the meter says no accent should go. It presupposes the meter is the ground and the syncopation is a figure against it. In the standard pattern, there is no privileged downbeat. The pattern *is* the time. If you accent its strokes, you are accenting the structure, not deviating from it. Gerhard Kubik, across decades of work culminating in *Theory of African Music* (Univ. of Chicago, 2010), has insisted on this distinction. The pattern is generative, not derivative.

What the pattern *generates* is a field. Support drums, rattles, hand-claps, master-drum improvisation lock into the field, each at its own offset, none required to share the same downbeat. John Miller Chernoff, in *African Rhythm and African Sensibility* (Univ. of Chicago, 1979), argued that this is fundamentally a *social* phenomenon. You cannot play these rhythms alone. The polyrhythm exists in the gap between the parts. Chernoff describes the "metronome sense" Ewe drummers develop — an internal pulse so steady the player can deliberately play *off* it without losing it. That capacity is the fundamental skill, and it is the same skill that will reappear, transposed, in every downstream tradition this essay touches.

## Specific traditions, named

The standard pattern is one timeline, not the only one. Reducing the West and Central African scene to a single bell pattern is exactly the move Agawu warns against. Some of the practices the music historically draws from:

### Ewe drumming (Ghana, Togo)

The Anlo-Ewe ensemble model is bell-as-timekeeper, master-drum-as-improviser. The *gankogui* (forged double iron bell) carries the timeline. The *axatse* (gourd rattle in a beaded net) doubles or counter-states it. Support drums — *kagan*, *kidi* — lock into offset ostinati. The master drum (*sogo*, or the four-and-a-half-foot *atsimevu*) cues changes and improvises. Repertoire: Agbadza, Atsiagbekor or Agbekor, Gahu. On record: *Ewe Music of Ghana* (Smithsonian Folkways), *African and Afro-American Drums* (FW04500). Locke's *Agbadza: The Critical Edition* is open-access; he is candid in it that "staff notation is an alien visualization system for African music."

### Yoruba batá (Nigeria, Cuba)

Three drums — *Iyá* (mother, master), *Itótele* (middle), *Okónkolo* (smallest). The architecture differs from the Ewe model in a structurally interesting way: the Okónkolo is the smallest drum and is *itself* the timeline. No separate bell. The smallest drum holds the time. Sacred to Orisha worship in Yorubaland; in Cuba, batá became central to the Lucumí / Santería complex, and the Cuban tradition has preserved liturgical practices warfare and colonial pressure interrupted in 19th-century Yorubaland. The diaspora archive, here, is more elaborate than the homeland survival. *Sacred Rhythms of Cuban Santería* (Smithsonian Folkways SF40419).

### Mandé djembe and dunun (Mali, Guinea)

Goblet-shaped djembe paired with three barrel-shaped *dunun* — *dununba*, *sangban*, *kenkeni*. Each dunun has a bell mounted on it; the *sangban* typically carries the key pattern. Repertoire: Dundunba ("dance of the strong men," Hamana region of Guinea), Kuku, Kassa. Modern documentarians: Mamady Keïta, Famoudou Konaté. Konaté's *Rhythmen der Malinke* (Museum Collection Berlin CD-18, 1991).

### Aka / BaAka (Central African Republic)

Interlocking yodeled vocal polyphony built on *hocket* — each singer contributes one or two pitches at precise time-slots, and the resultant melody emerges from the composite, audible to everyone but played by no one. The same hocket logic governs the percussion. Simha Arom's *African Polyphony and Polyrhythm* (Cambridge, 1991) shows that what sounds improvisational is highly structured around fixed model-variation pairs. György Ligeti wrote the foreword and explicitly cited Aka structure for his late piano études (*Désordre*, *Fanfares*). The 2003 Teldec disc *African Rhythms* (Aimard with Aka singers) places Ligeti, Reich, and Aka field recordings side by side. (One correction: Reich's African studies were with the Ewe drummer Gideon Alorwoyie in Ghana in 1970, not Aka. The Aka connection is Ligeti's.) Field recordings: *Centrafrique: Anthologie de la musique des Pygmées Aka* (Ocora).

### Shona mbira (Zimbabwe)

The *mbira dzavadzimu* — "mbira of the ancestors" — has 22 to 28 metal keys on a wooden soundboard, played inside a calabash resonator with bottle-cap rattles for buzz. Played at all-night *bira* spirit-possession ceremonies. Two players interlock: *kushaura* ("to lead") and *kutsinhira* ("to follow"). 48-pulse phrase cycle. Listeners reliably hear "inherent" melodies no single player is producing — psychoacoustic gestalt grouping segregating the composite into apparent independent streams. Paul Berliner's *The Soul of Mbira* (Univ. of California, 1978); the companion record (Nonesuch Explorer H-72054, 1973). Canonical piece: Nhemamusasa.

### Senegambian sabar (Senegal, Gambia)

The Wolof sabar tradition uses a hand-and-stick technique on a high-pitched single-headed drum. Doudou N'Diaye Rose, the mathematician of rhythm, is credited with developing some five hundred patterns. *The Doudou N'Diaye Rose Family — Twenty-One Sabar Rhythms* (Soul Jazz, 2022).

These traditions do not share a single rhythmic signature. They share a *temperament* — a willingness to build music out of multiple co-equal pulses, a tendency to externalize the timekeeping role into a fixed asymmetric pattern, a comfort with hocket and interlocking, a privileging of ensemble interaction over any single soloist's relationship to a metric grid. That temperament is what crossed the Atlantic.

## The Atlantic crossing: clave as structural axis

The clave is the most consequential single rhythmic figure in the music of the Americas. Not a "Latin flavor" — its own organizational principle, on par with the standard pattern from which it descends, redistributed onto a sixteen-pulse duple grid.

### The anatomy

**Son clave**, in 4/4, places strokes on the downbeat of bar 1, the "a" of beat 1, the "&" of beat 2, the "&" of beat 3, and the downbeat of beat 4 of bar 2 — five strokes straddling two bars. The first half, the *three-side*, is internally a 3+3+2 grouping of sixteenth-note pulses. That 3+3+2 cell anchors the Bo Diddley beat. It is the cell the habanera bass figure rocks back on. It is everywhere.

**Rumba clave** differs at exactly one position: the third stroke of the three-side is pushed one sixteenth-note later — to the "a" of beat 2 instead of the "&" of beat 2. That single displacement pulls the pattern further off the metric grid and gives it the looser, longer-feeling tension rumba players treat as the more "African" placement.

**6/8 clave** — *bembé* — is the triple-pulse ancestor, closely related to the West African standard pattern. Son and rumba clave descend from it when you collapse twelve pulses to sixteen and reweight the asymmetry.

### The structural role

The clave is not a backing pattern. It is a structural axis. Cuban music is "in clave" or "out of clave" the way English is in or out of grammatical agreement. A melody whose accents cross into the wrong half is *cruzado* — it crosses wrong — and Cuban musicians hear it instantly, the way an English speaker hears a misplaced word stress. The clave has direction: songs are in *3-2 clave* if the three-side comes first, *2-3 clave* if the two-side comes first. Horn lines, piano *montuno* patterns, bass *tumbao* lines are written to fit one direction or the other.

A caveat. The "3-2 vs 2-3" terminology is a pedagogical convention from non-Cuban codification — most insistent in NYC mambo-era teaching and Latin-jazz pedagogy. Some Cuban musicians reject the labels as a foreign artifact. Mongo Santamaría dismissed them; the bassist Alain Pérez has said something close to: *Don't tell me about 3-2 or 2-3. In Cuba, we just play.* The phenomenon is real; the labels are an outsider convention. The clave is the duple-pulse cousin of the standard pattern. What changed in the crossing is the pulse subdivision (twelve to sixteen) and the cultural environment in which the pattern took root.

### The Cuban genres, briefly

- ***Son cubano***. Eastern Cuba, late 19th century, codified around 1920. Tres, bongos, claves, vocals, marímbula or contrabass. Trío Matamoros, Sexteto Habanero, Septeto Nacional. The matrix.
- ***Rumba***. Tenement courtyards (*solares*) of Havana and Matanzas. Three subgenres: *yambú* (slowest, oldest, originally on cajones), *guaguancó* (medium, conga-driven, structured around the *vacunao*), *columbia* (fastest, 6/8, clearest line back to West African rural roots). Conjunto Folklórico Nacional; Los Muñequitos de Matanzas.
- ***Mambo***. Three stages: Orestes López's 1938 *danzón* "Mambo"; Pérez Prado's 1940s international scaling; the 1950s Palladium era in NYC, with Machito, Tito Puente, Tito Rodríguez — the Three Kings. Puente's *Dance Mania* (RCA, recorded November–December 1957).
- ***Cha-cha-chá***. Enrique Jorrín with Orquesta América, 1953. "La Engañadora."
- ***Salsa***. 1970s NYC. Cuban son montuno crossed with Puerto Rican input, codified through Fania. Palmieri, Colón, Lavoe.
- ***Timba***. Late 1980s, Special Period Cuba. Los Van Van (Formell, 1969); NG La Banda (Cortés, 1988; *En la calle*, 1990).

Afro-Cuba is not one thing. The religious and folkloric substrate is plural: the Yoruba-derived **Lucumí** complex (batá, Santería, the *Oru del Igbodu* sequence); the **Carabalí** strand from Cross River and Efik peoples, source of the Abakuá initiation society (founded in Cuba in 1836); the **Arará** complex (Fon, Dahomey-derived), strongest in Matanzas; the **Kongo / Bantu** strand — Palo, Regla de Congo — from the BaKongo, who between 1760 and 1790 were roughly 30% of the African-born enslaved population of Cuba, the single largest *nación*. Each has its own drums, patterns, ritual logic. Fernando Ortiz, in *La Africanía de la Música Folklórica de Cuba* (1950) and earlier in *Contrapunteo cubano del tabaco y el azúcar* (1940), where he coined *transculturación*, made these distinctions legible as Cuban national heritage rather than as something to be effaced.

## The bridge into jazz

Two names carry most of the weight of how Afro-Cuban rhythmic organization entered jazz: Mario Bauzá and Chano Pozo.

**Mario Bauzá** (1911–1993), Havana-born, classically trained on clarinet and oboe, arrived in NYC in 1930. He played in the Chick Webb and Cab Calloway big bands; in 1939 he co-founded Machito and his Afro-Cubans with his brother-in-law Frank Grillo (Machito), and served as musical director. The piece widely cited as the first true Afro-Cuban jazz composition is Bauzá's "Tanga," first performed at a Park Palace Ballroom rehearsal on May 29, 1943 — what happens when a Cuban musical director with classical training and big-band swing experience foregrounds the clave under an improvising horn line.

**Chano Pozo** (Luciano Pozo González, born January 7, 1915, Havana; died December 2, 1948, Harlem) was a conguero with deep roots in Havana's Abakuá and rumba scenes. Bauzá introduced him to Dizzy Gillespie in 1947. The window is short and the consequences are large:

- September 29, 1947: Carnegie Hall debut with the Gillespie big band — first performance of "Manteca."
- December 22, 1947: studio recording of "Manteca" for RCA Victor (Gillespie / Pozo / Gil Fuller). The moment Afro-Cuban jazz becomes audible on record at the highest level of jazz production.
- December 1947: George Russell's "Cubana Be / Cubana Bop" recorded with Pozo.
- "Tin Tin Deo," another Gillespie / Pozo collaboration, enters the standard repertoire.

Pozo was killed at the El Rio Bar at 111th and Lenox by Eusebio "Cabito" Muñoz, roughly a year after the Carnegie debut. The 1947–1948 window — eighteen months — is short enough that it is striking how foundational it is. Most of what is now called Afro-Cuban jazz points back to it.

**Mongo Santamaría** (1917–2003), Cuban conguero working with Cal Tjader's group, composed "Afro Blue," first recorded April 20, 1959 at the Sunset Auditorium in Carmel. The first jazz standard built explicitly on the African 6/8 cross-rhythm — three in the bass against two in the melody — rather than on swung 4/4. Coltrane recorded it 1963. The standard pattern's hemiola interval, planted in the jazz songbook.

Two upstream names. **Arsenio Rodríguez** (1911–1970), the blind tres player called *El Ciego Maravilloso*, formalized the *conjunto* — piano in place of guitar, added conga, expanded horns — and codified son montuno into the template that became salsa. **Tito Puente**, *El Rey del Timbal*, was the major NYC mambo big-band leader of the same era.

## New Orleans: where the two pipelines meet

The Cuban pipeline is the most visible route of African rhythmic logic into US music. It is not the only route. There is a parallel, direct line — slave trade into the Anglophone South, a partial preservation of practice in a few specific sites — and the city where the two lines meet is New Orleans.

### Congo Square

Two dates, with a correction to a frequently repeated wrong one. **1817**: city ordinances restricting gatherings of enslaved people to Sunday afternoons in the Place Publique — Congo Square. Benjamin Latrobe's journal entry for February 21, 1819, is the primary contemporary witness, describing *bamboula* drums and African dance circles. **1856**: a comprehensive city ban on people of African descent playing horns or drums anywhere within the city. The Sunday gatherings had effectively shut down by around 1851. (The date sometimes cited as the cutoff, 1843, was an infrastructure year — iron chains and posts replaced wooden railings. Not a ban year.)

What Congo Square preserved, however imperfectly, was a site where African ensemble practice could be maintained: drums, dance, call-and-response, the social context in which polyrhythm exists. One of the few documented antebellum sites in the US where this preservation was even possible. Most of the South suppressed African drumming after the 1739 Stono Rebellion in South Carolina, in which drums had been used to coordinate. The Louisiana legal regime — French, then Spanish, briefly French again, then American — was distinctive enough that suppression came later and incompletely.

### The Spanish tinge

Ned Sublette's *The World That Made New Orleans: From Spanish Silver to Congo Square* (Lawrence Hill, 2008) is the indispensable text on how Caribbean trade, Spanish colonial governance, and Cuban contact made New Orleans the rhythmic crossroads it became. The *habanera* bass figure — a dotted-eighth, sixteenth, and two eighths cell — entered through Spanish colonial and Cuban contact in the 19th century. It is the rhythmic skeleton of "St. Louis Blues" (Handy, 1914). It is what Jelly Roll Morton meant on the 1938 Library of Congress recordings with Alan Lomax: *If you can't manage to put tinges of Spanish in your tunes, you will never be able to get the right seasoning, I call it, for jazz.*

The point. New Orleans is where the Cuban pipeline and the direct-African line meet. The Spanish tinge is, technically, a Cuban import. But it lands on a city already drumming Africanly in Congo Square. The two lines arrive at the same ear.

### Baby Dodds and the kit

The translation of polyrhythmic ensemble practice onto the drum kit happens in New Orleans, and the central figure is **Warren "Baby" Dodds** (1898–1959). Dodds invented the *big four* — a cymbal accent on beat four of every other bar, which broke the two-beat march pattern and turned it toward jazz swing. He was the first jazz drummer to make the bass drum's rhythmic undercurrent the foundation of the kit's groove, audible on his October 1927 sessions, and among the first to convert the press-roll pattern into the basic ride-cymbal beat. The kit becomes a way to play polyrhythm with limbs — bell-equivalent in one hand, cross-rhythm in the other, downbeat in the foot.

The brass-band tradition is the unbroken thread from antebellum to present. Olympia Brass Band (Tremé, late 19th century). Dirty Dozen Brass Band (Benny Jones, 1977; *My Feet Can't Fail Me Now*, 1984, sparked the modern renaissance). Rebirth Brass Band (Phil Frazier, Keith Frazier, Kermit Ruffins, 1983, Joseph S. Clark Senior High in Tremé). The second-line parade — participatory dancers and players following the main line — is the living social form in which the polyrhythmic ensemble logic is preserved as everyday practice.

## The direct-African line: blues, gospel, funk

Outside New Orleans, the question of African retention in US Black music is more contested. Suppression of drumming after Stono was widespread enough that the most visible African ensemble practices did not survive intact across most of the South. What survived, and where, is Samuel Floyd's central question in *The Power of Black Music* (Oxford, 1995).

### Floyd's argument

Floyd plants his flag on the **ring shout** as the master site of African retention in the US — a counter-clockwise shuffling circle dance, hand-clapping and call-and-response, that survived among the Gullah / Geechee of the coastal Sea Islands of South Carolina and Georgia. The shuffle without crossing of feet was a survival strategy: it permitted the dance under Protestant prohibitions on "dancing," because technically no foot crossed another. The McIntosh County Shouters and Griffin Lotson's Gullah Geechee Ring Shouters out of Crescent, Georgia, are active living-tradition bearers.

Floyd's claim: the ring shout is the practice from which the entire vocabulary of US Black music radiates — *calls, cries, hollers, call-and-response devices, additive rhythms and polyrhythms.* He borrows Henry Louis Gates Jr.'s **Signifyin(g)** and recasts call-and-response as the *master trope* — the trope of tropes. If the West African standard pattern is the cell from which Cuban clave descends, the ring shout is the cell from which Black US music descends.

### Robert Farris Thompson and the cool

Robert Farris Thompson (1932–2021), in *Flash of the Spirit* (Random House, 1983), maps **five** African civilizations onto diasporic practice: **Yoruba, Kongo, Ejagham, Mande, Cross River**. The Thompson concept that has carried furthest into how musicians talk about the music is the **Aesthetic of the Cool**, descended from the Yoruba *itutu* — "mystic coolness," tranquility of mind, generosity, conciliation, the "frozen face" as marker of spiritual depth. Rhythmically, it resolves to a single virtue: *the ability to hold the time-line steady while everything else moves.* That is the bedrock virtue of a good drummer or bassist in Black American music. It is the same virtue Chernoff's Ewe drummers cultivate — the metronome sense — in a different idiom.

### Olly Wilson and heterogeneity

Olly Wilson's 1992 essay "The Heterogeneous Sound Ideal in African-American Music" is the third corner. Wilson argues that Afro-diasporic music has a fundamental bias for *heterogeneity of sound* — timbral contrast, rough-edged textures, body-movement integration, listener participation. It is why Black American music tends toward distorted guitars, growled saxes, hand-claps that do not sit on the grid, the *buzz* of mbira bottle-caps. Smoothness is not the goal. The aesthetic prizes the audible texture of human presence.

### Kubik's blues thesis (his argument, not consensus)

Gerhard Kubik, in *Africa and the Blues* (Univ. Press of Mississippi, 1999), argues that the African traits in blues come predominantly from the savanna and Sahel zones — Mali, Niger, Mauritania — rather than from the forest belt that supplied most of the standard-pattern traditions. He identifies what he calls "Arabic-Islamic style characteristics" in Delta blues — particular ornament patterns, scalar inflections, vocal melisma — as evidence of Sahelian and Mande lineage. Influential and contested. Worth flagging as Kubik's argument, not consensus. What it does usefully is partition the African heritage into multiple specific sources rather than collapsing them into a generic origin story.

### Hooker, boogie, the one-man polyrhythm

John Lee Hooker's "Boogie Chillen'" (1948, Modern) is the cleanest one-man demonstration of the African polyrhythmic principle on US soil. Hooker's guitar plays a riff. His foot stomps a steady pulse, captured by a microphone in a wooden pallet. His voice cuts across both. The foot is the externalized timeline. Charles Shaar Murray called it a "jumping, polyrhythmic groove which he learned in the Delta." Recorded with primitive echo via a speaker in a toilet bowl. The economic and technological constraint is total. The principle is intact.

The boogie-woogie ostinato — eight to the bar in the left hand, syncopated figures in the right — is the same principle on a piano. (Etymology unsettled: Hausa *Boog*, Mandingo *Booga*, Bantu *Mbuki Mvuki* are candidates in the literature, suggestive rather than confirmed.)

### Gospel, the church, the clap

Gospel and the Black church inherit the ring-shout substrate and add the European Protestant hymn tradition on top. The hand-clap on beats 2 and 4 — the "backbeat" — is the form's most ubiquitous rhythmic feature. Mellonee Burnim's "The Black Gospel Music Tradition: Symbol of Ethnicity" (Indiana, 1980) is the canonical academic text. The 2-and-4 clap is itself a polyrhythmic act: it crosses the 1-and-3 emphasis of European hymn meter, two pulses coexisting — the same logic as the bell pattern, transposed onto a four-square Protestant frame.

### James Brown, "Cold Sweat," the one

James Brown's "Cold Sweat" (May 1967, King Records) is the record most often cited as the first true funk recording. It has only one definite chord change. Jerry Wexler said of it: *It deeply affected the musicians I knew. It just freaked them out. No one could get a handle on what to do next.* Grammy Hall of Fame, 2016. What "Cold Sweat" formalized was a way of organizing music in which harmonic motion is suspended and the rhythmic interlock becomes the entirety of the form. Bass, guitar scratch, horn stabs, drum kit, vocal — each a part in the ensemble, each with its own offset, locked to a shared timeline.

*On the one* — JB's emphasis on beat 1 as the locus of return, against the rock and R&B convention of accenting beats 2 and 4 — is the slogan version of this organization. Beat 1 is *home*. The polyrhythmic web circles back to it. Put on "Funky Drummer" (1970) and pay attention to where the ensemble *lands*. It is not where the snare hits.

(One framing point worth marking as analogy rather than as sourced ethnomusicology: when Indian classical pedagogy returns to *sam* — the first beat of the *tala* cycle, the arrival point the improvisation circles back to — the conceptual job *sam* does is similar to the conceptual job *the one* does in funk. That analogy is mine. We will pick it up properly in Part 3.)

The most rigorous academic treatment of funk's micro-timing is **Anne Danielsen**'s *Presence and Pleasure: The Funk Grooves of James Brown and Parliament* (Wesleyan, 2006) — what is sitting where, how far ahead or behind the grid, how the *pleasure* of the groove is produced by these displacements. This is exactly category (c) from Part 1, applied to the music that made the category urgent.

### The Meters, go-go, house

The Meters' "Cissy Strut" (1969), Joseph "Zigaboo" Modeliste on drums, is one of the most-sampled drum grooves in recorded music. Modeliste sits between straight and swung sixteenth-notes — variable displacement, never quite quantized — and emphasizes the last half-beat of the bar. Second-line syncopation transposed into funk. You cannot quantize the groove without killing it.

DC go-go, founded by Chuck Brown (1936–2012) in the early 1970s, is a slightly different lineage. Brown played with Los Latinos, a local Latin band, before forming the Soul Searchers. The congas-plus-trap-kit interlock that defines go-go comes from explicit Afro-Cuban contact, not pure direct-African retention. "Bustin' Loose" (1978) was a #1 R&B record. Go-go is one of the cleanest demonstrations that the Cuban pipeline and the direct-African line continued to interfuse downstream from New Orleans.

House music in Chicago in the early 1980s, as codified at the Warehouse by Frankie Knuckles, is partly a 4-on-the-floor flattening of polyrhythmic complexity for dance-floor utility. (Whether to call disco's 4-on-the-floor a "Western flattening" of Afro-Cuban polyrhythm is an interpretive move. Mine. Not established musicology.) But house also carries the gospel inheritance forward. Knuckles's own description of house was *a church for the children fallen from grace.* Many early Chicago producers were grandchildren of Black Southerners who had come north in the Great Migration, carrying gospel and blues sensibility into electronic-dance form. That house is also the genre in which a programmable swing parameter — the Linn / MPC swing — becomes a producer's daily decision is the bridge to Part 4.

## Three listening exercises

The point is to put the bell in your ear — not as a concept, but as a reference your body can hold against everything else.

### Exercise 1: The standard pattern in three readings

Find a recording of Anlo-Ewe Agbadza. *Ewe Music of Ghana* (Smithsonian Folkways) or *African and Afro-American Drums* (FW04500). Listen for the *gankogui* — highest-pitched, most metallic-cutting, the iron bell. Seven strokes across twelve pulses.

Now, separately:

1. Tap four with your foot — each foot-tap a dotted-quarter beat. The bell does not align evenly. Hold the foot pulse anyway. (Compound-duple reading.)
2. Tap six with your foot — each foot-tap a quarter note. The bell still does not align evenly. Hold this pulse anyway. (Simple-triple reading.)
3. Try to hold both at once. Three in one hand, four in the other, the bell on top. The point is not that you will succeed. The point is that the music is built to support all of these readings simultaneously.

The same exercise works on the Yoruba bembé bell, on Cuban 6/8 rumba, on "Afro Blue" (Coltrane 1963, with Elvin Jones playing the cross-rhythm explicitly).

### Exercise 2: The clave under things that aren't "Latin"

Put on Bo Diddley's "Bo Diddley" (Checker, 1955). The riff figure — *bomp, ba-bomp-bomp, ba-bomp-bomp* — is the three-side of son clave: 3+3+2 in sixteenth-notes. Now Buddy Holly's "Not Fade Away" (Brunswick, 1957), a direct lift. Then The Who's "Magic Bus" (Decca, 1968), same cell. Then "St. Louis Blues" (any 1920s recording; Handy wrote it 1914). The habanera bass figure under the verses is the Spanish tinge — Cuban import in NYC and Memphis blues by 1914. Once you can hear the cell, it does not stop.

### Exercise 3: Modeliste against the grid, Hooker against his foot

Put on "Cissy Strut" (The Meters, 1969). Set a metronome at tempo, four beats per bar. Where do Modeliste's snare and hi-hat actually land relative to the click? Not on the grid — against it, variable displacement that is part of the groove's identity.

Now John Lee Hooker's "Boogie Chillen'" (Modern, 1948). The foot-stomp is the timeline. Guitar and voice ride against it. Try to hold the foot-stomp in your head while listening to where the guitar phrases fall. They do not align in any quantized way. They do not need to. The foot is the clock.

You have just heard category (c) and category (b) in one sitting, both descended from the same temperament.

## Closing the loop

The argument of this essay, condensed: the rhythmic temperament of Black American music is not a stylistic flavor laid on top of a European meter. It is a different organizational logic for time, drawn from specific West and Central African ensemble practices — Ewe, Yoruba, Mandé, Aka, Shona, Wolof — in which an asymmetric, externalized timeline is the structural axis around which the rest of the music is built. That logic crossed the Atlantic in two flows: through Cuba, where it organized son, rumba, mambo, and salsa around the *clave*; and through direct retention into the US, where it survived most visibly in the ring shout, the New Orleans drumming culture, the Black church, and the brass-band tradition. The two flows met in New Orleans, and from that meeting jazz, blues, gospel, funk, and everything downstream draws its time-feel.

The Agawu correction stays operative: the patterns are specific, the traditions are plural, "African rhythm" is a colonial framing that should not survive its own examination. What survives is a careful inventory of named practices and the recognition that the temperament they share — multiple co-equal pulses, externalized timeline, ensemble interlock, the metronome sense, the cool — has organized more of the 20th century's recorded music than any other rhythmic disposition on Earth.

Two threads remain.

**Part 3** picks up Indian classical applications. Konnakol — the South Indian solfège-for-rhythm in which spoken syllables (*ta, ka, di, mi, ta, ka, ta, ki, ta*) encode rhythmic phrases. The *tala* cycle — *Adi* (eight beats), *Rupaka* (six), longer cycles like *Ata* and *Dhruva* (both fourteen, organized differently) — is the framework in which an externalized cyclic reference is treated as structural axis, the way the bell pattern is in Ewe practice. *Sam*, the first beat of the cycle, is the arrival point improvisation circles back to. The Carnatic vocabulary Coltrane glanced at in *Om* and *India*, and that Rudresh Mahanthappa now improvises in fluently after studying konnakol with Subash Chandran, is the next leg. We will pay close attention to what *resonates* between the West African and South Indian frames (cyclical timeline, externalized reference, *sam* and *the one* as structural arrival) and what is genuinely different (gati, jati, the tihai cadence, the way Indian rhythm is *spoken* before it is played).

**Part 4** picks up the electronic thread. The Linn LM-1 swing parameter (Roger Linn, 1980), the MPC60 swing (Akai/Linn, 1988), the DAW swing controls that descended from them. Where Dilla's 50–62% swing setting fits in the picture. What modern producers — Madlib, Karriem Riggins, Kaytranada, MIKE — are doing with the inheritance in environments where the timeline is now a piece of software and the displacement is a knob.

There is more music waiting on the other side of the bell pattern than there was on this one. We will get there.
