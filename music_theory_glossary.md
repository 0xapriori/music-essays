## 1. Pitch & Notation Fundamentals

- **Pitch** — How high or low a sound is, determined by its fundamental frequency (measured in Hz).
- **Note** — A named musical sound of definite pitch and duration.
- **Tone / Semitone** — A semitone (half step) is the smallest interval in Western music; a tone (whole step) equals two semitones.
- **Octave** — The interval between one pitch and another with double (or half) its frequency. Notes an octave apart share the same letter name and sound "the same but higher/lower" (octave equivalence).
- **Pitch class** — All pitches sharing a name regardless of octave (every C is in pitch class C).
- **Chromatic scale** — All 12 pitches within an octave, each a semitone apart.
- **Enharmonic equivalents** — Two names for the same pitch (e.g., F♯ = G♭).
- **Accidentals** — Symbols altering a pitch: sharp (♯, +1 semitone), flat (♭, −1 semitone), natural (♮, cancel), double-sharp (𝄪), double-flat (𝄫).
- **Staff (stave)** — The five lines and four spaces on which notes are written.
- **Clef** — Symbol fixing pitch names to staff lines: treble (G), bass (F), alto/tenor (C).
- **Ledger lines** — Short lines extending the staff for notes above or below it.
- **Middle C** — C4, the C near the center of the piano, a common reference pitch.
- **Concert pitch** — The standard tuning reference, A4 = 440 Hz (A440).
- **Register** — The relative highness/lowness of a range of pitches (e.g., a singer's chest vs. head register).

## 2. Intervals

- **Interval** — The distance in pitch between two notes.
- **Melodic vs. harmonic interval** — Notes played in sequence (melodic) vs. simultaneously (harmonic).
- **Interval quality** — The category that refines size: **perfect, major, minor, augmented, diminished**.
- **Perfect intervals** — Unison, 4th, 5th, octave (acoustically pure, stable).
- **Major/minor intervals** — 2nds, 3rds, 6ths, 7ths come in major (larger) and minor (one semitone smaller) forms.
- **Augmented / diminished** — A perfect or major interval widened by a semitone is augmented; narrowed is diminished.
- **Common interval sizes** (in semitones):
  - Minor 2nd (1), Major 2nd (2)
  - Minor 3rd (3), Major 3rd (4)
  - Perfect 4th (5)
  - Tritone / Augmented 4th / Diminished 5th (6)
  - Perfect 5th (7)
  - Minor 6th (8), Major 6th (9)
  - Minor 7th (10), Major 7th (11)
  - Octave (12)
- **Tritone** — The interval of six semitones (three whole tones); historically called *diabolus in musica*, central to tension and dominant harmony.
- **Compound interval** — An interval larger than an octave (e.g., a 9th = octave + 2nd).
- **Inversion (of an interval)** — Flipping the lower note above the upper; intervals and their inversions sum to 9 (a 3rd inverts to a 6th).
- **Consonance vs. dissonance** — Intervals perceived as stable/restful (consonant) vs. tense/needing resolution (dissonant).

## 3. Scales & Modes

- **Scale** — An ordered collection of pitches ascending/descending within an octave.
- **Major scale** — Pattern of whole/half steps: W–W–H–W–W–W–H. The reference for tonal music.
- **Minor scales:**
  - **Natural minor** — W–H–W–W–H–W–W (the Aeolian mode).
  - **Harmonic minor** — Natural minor with a raised 7th (creates a leading tone).
  - **Melodic minor** — Raised 6th and 7th ascending, often natural minor descending.
- **Pentatonic scale** — Five-note scale; major (C–D–E–G–A) and minor variants, common in folk, blues, rock.
- **Blues scale** — The minor pentatonic plus an added "blue note," the flatted 5th (♭5). (Other blue notes, such as the ♭3, are expressive pitch inflections rather than added scale members.)
- **Modes (church/diatonic modes)** — Seven scales derived from the major scale starting on different degrees:
  - **Ionian** (major), **Dorian**, **Phrygian**, **Lydian**, **Mixolydian**, **Aeolian** (natural minor), **Locrian**.
- **Whole-tone scale** — Six notes all a whole step apart; symmetrical, "floating" sound.
- **Diminished / octatonic scale** — Alternating whole and half steps (8 notes).
- **Scale degree** — Position of a note in a scale, numbered 1–7 and named: tonic (1), supertonic (2), mediant (3), subdominant (4), dominant (5), submediant (6), leading tone / subtonic (7).
- **Tetrachord** — A four-note segment spanning a perfect 4th; scales are often built from two tetrachords.

## 4. Keys & Tonality

- **Key** — The tonal center (tonic) and scale a piece is based around.
- **Tonic** — The home pitch/chord that feels like rest and resolution.
- **Key signature** — Sharps or flats at the start of a staff defining the key.
- **Circle of fifths** — Diagram arranging the 12 keys by ascending perfect 5ths; shows key relationships and accumulating sharps/flats.
- **Relative keys** — A major and minor key sharing a key signature (C major / A minor).
- **Parallel keys** — A major and minor key sharing a tonic (C major / C minor).
- **Modulation** — Changing key within a piece.
- **Transposition** — Shifting all pitches by a fixed interval into another key.
- **Diatonic vs. chromatic** — Notes belonging to the current key (diatonic) vs. notes outside it (chromatic).
- **Tonality vs. atonality** — Music organized around a tonic (tonal) vs. music deliberately avoiding a tonal center (atonal).

## 5. Chords & Harmony

- **Chord** — Three or more pitches sounded together.
- **Harmony** — The combination and progression of simultaneous pitches.
- **Triad** — A three-note chord built in thirds: root, third, fifth. Four qualities:
  - **Major** (major 3rd + minor 3rd)
  - **Minor** (minor 3rd + major 3rd)
  - **Diminished** (two minor 3rds)
  - **Augmented** (two major 3rds)
- **Root** — The note a chord is built on and named after.
- **Seventh chords** — Triad plus a seventh: major 7th, dominant 7th, minor 7th, half-diminished, diminished 7th, minor-major 7th.
- **Extended chords** — Adding 9ths, 11ths, 13ths above the seventh.
- **Added-tone chords** — e.g., add9, 6/9 (extra notes without stacking sevenths).
- **Suspended chords (sus2, sus4)** — Third replaced by a 2nd or 4th, creating unresolved tension.
- **Inversion (of a chord)** — Which chord tone is in the bass: root position, first inversion (3rd in bass), second inversion (5th in bass), etc.
- **Voicing** — The specific vertical spacing and ordering of a chord's notes.
- **Arpeggio** — A chord played one note at a time rather than simultaneously.
- **Power chord** — Root + fifth (no third); common in rock, neither major nor minor.
- **Figured bass** — Baroque shorthand using numbers below a bass line to indicate chord inversions/intervals.
- **Lead sheet / chord symbols** — Notation like Cmaj7, Dm, G7, F♯dim used in jazz/pop.

## 6. Harmonic Function & Progression

- **Chord progression** — A sequence of chords forming the harmonic backbone of a passage.
- **Roman numeral analysis** — Labeling chords by scale degree (I, ii, iii, IV, V, vi, vii°); uppercase = major, lowercase = minor.
- **Harmonic function** — A chord's role:
  - **Tonic (T)** — rest/home (I, vi).
  - **Subdominant / pre-dominant (S)** — motion away (IV, ii).
  - **Dominant (D)** — tension demanding resolution (V, vii°).
- **Cadence** — A harmonic close to a phrase:
  - **Authentic (V–I)** — strong resolution.
  - **Plagal (IV–I)** — the "amen" cadence.
  - **Half (… –V)** — ends on the dominant, unresolved.
  - **Deceptive (V–vi)** — resolution dodged.
- **Voice leading** — How individual voices move smoothly from chord to chord.
- **Secondary dominant** — A dominant chord borrowed to tonicize a non-tonic chord (e.g., V/V).
- **Borrowed chord / modal mixture** — Chords taken from the parallel key.
- **Tritone substitution** — Replacing a dominant chord with one a tritone away (jazz).
- **Pedal point** — A sustained note (often bass) held while harmonies change above it.
- **Tension and resolution** — The core dynamic of dissonance moving to consonance.

## 7. Rhythm, Meter & Tempo

- **Rhythm** — The pattern of durations and accents in time.
- **Beat / pulse** — The steady underlying unit of time.
- **Tempo** — Speed of the beat, in BPM or Italian terms (Largo, Adagio, Andante, Moderato, Allegro, Presto).
- **Meter** — The grouping of beats into recurring patterns.
- **Time signature** — Two numbers showing beats per measure (top) and the note value of the beat (bottom), e.g., 4/4, 3/4, 6/8.
- **Simple vs. compound meter** — Beats divided into 2 (simple) vs. 3 (compound).
- **Duple, triple, quadruple meter** — 2, 3, or 4 beats per measure.
- **Measure (bar)** — A segment of time containing a set number of beats, divided by barlines.
- **Note values** — Whole, half, quarter, eighth, sixteenth, etc.; each half the duration of the previous.
- **Rest** — Notated silence, with durations mirroring note values.
- **Dotted note** — A dot adds half the note's value again.
- **Tie** — Joins two notes into one sustained duration.
- **Syncopation** — Accenting off-beats or weak beats against the meter.
- **Tuplet (e.g., triplet)** — Dividing a beat into an irregular number of equal parts.
- **Polyrhythm** — Two or more conflicting rhythms played simultaneously (e.g., 3 against 2).
- **Anacrusis (pickup)** — Note(s) before the first downbeat.
- **Groove / feel** — The cumulative rhythmic character (e.g., swing vs. straight).
- **Swing** — Uneven (long–short) subdivision of the beat.

## 8. Melody & Counterpoint

- **Melody** — A succession of pitches perceived as a single coherent line.
- **Motif (motive)** — A short, recurring melodic/rhythmic idea.
- **Phrase** — A musical "sentence," often 4 or 8 bars, ending in a cadence.
- **Theme** — A complete, memorable melodic idea that serves as source material.
- **Contour** — The overall shape (rise and fall) of a melodic line.
- **Conjunct vs. disjunct motion** — Stepwise motion vs. motion by leaps.
- **Counterpoint** — The art of combining independent melodic lines.
- **Polyphony** — Texture of multiple simultaneous independent melodies.
- **Imitation** — One voice restating a melodic idea introduced by another.
- **Canon / round** — Strict imitation where voices enter successively (e.g., "Row, Row, Row Your Boat").
- **Fugue** — A contrapuntal form built on a subject developed through imitation across voices.
- **Ornamentation** — Decorative notes: trill, mordent, turn, grace note, appoggiatura.
- **Sequence** — Repeating a melodic pattern at successively higher or lower pitches.

## 9. Dynamics, Articulation & Expression

- **Dynamics** — Loudness levels: *pp* (pianissimo), *p* (piano), *mp*, *mf*, *f* (forte), *ff* (fortissimo).
- **Crescendo / diminuendo (decrescendo)** — Gradually getting louder / softer.
- **Accent** — Emphasis on a particular note.
- **Articulation** — How notes are attacked and released:
  - **Legato** — smooth, connected.
  - **Staccato** — short, detached.
  - **Tenuto** — held to full value.
  - **Marcato** — strongly accented.
  - **Slur** — connect notes in one gesture/bow/breath.
- **Phrasing** — Shaping a line expressively, like punctuation in speech.
- **Rubato** — Expressive flexing of tempo.
- **Fermata** — Hold a note/rest longer than its written value.
- **Vibrato** — Slight, regular pitch fluctuation for warmth/expression.

## 10. Form & Structure

- **Form** — The large-scale organization of a piece.
- **Binary form (AB)** — Two contrasting sections.
- **Ternary form (ABA)** — Statement, contrast, return.
- **Rondo (ABACA…)** — A recurring refrain alternating with episodes.
- **Sonata form** — Exposition → development → recapitulation; the cornerstone of Classical instrumental music.
- **Theme and variations** — A theme followed by successive transformed restatements.
- **Strophic form** — Same music repeated for each verse (common in song/hymn).
- **Verse–chorus form** — Standard popular-song structure (verse, chorus, bridge).
- **Through-composed** — Continuous new material with little repetition.
- **Coda** — A concluding section rounding off a piece.
- **Intro / outro, bridge, refrain, hook** — Sectional building blocks in popular music.
- **Call and response** — Alternation between two phrases/groups (roots in African and gospel traditions).

## 11. Timbre & Texture

- **Timbre (tone color)** — The quality distinguishing instruments/voices at the same pitch and loudness.
- **Texture** — How many layers sound and how they relate:
  - **Monophony** — single melodic line, no harmony.
  - **Homophony** — melody with chordal accompaniment.
  - **Polyphony** — multiple independent melodic lines.
  - **Heterophony** — simultaneous variations of one melody.
- **Voicing / orchestration** — Distribution of pitches and parts among instruments.
- **Doubling** — Two parts playing the same line (often at the octave).
- **Register / range** — The span from lowest to highest available pitch of a voice or instrument.
- **Overtones / harmonics / partials** — The series of higher frequencies above a fundamental that define timbre.

## 12. Tuning & Acoustics

- **Frequency** — Cycles per second (Hz) determining pitch.
- **Harmonic series** — The natural sequence of overtones above a fundamental; the acoustic basis of intervals and timbre.
- **Equal temperament** — Standard modern tuning dividing the octave into 12 equal semitones.
- **Just intonation** — Tuning based on whole-number frequency ratios (pure intervals).
- **Pythagorean tuning** — Tuning derived from stacking pure perfect fifths.
- **Cent** — 1/100 of a semitone; the unit for measuring fine tuning differences.
- **A440** — The reference standard, A4 = 440 Hz.
- **Beats (acoustic)** — Pulsing interference heard when two close frequencies sound together; used for tuning.
- **Microtones** — Intervals smaller than a semitone, found in many world traditions.
- **Consonance/dissonance (acoustic basis)** — Simpler frequency ratios (2:1, 3:2) sound consonant; complex ratios sound dissonant.
