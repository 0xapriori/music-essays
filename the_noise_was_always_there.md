### How Industrial Sound Entered Hip-Hop and What It Revealed

*An essay on sonic extremity, racial epistemology, and the artists who rewired what rap is allowed to sound like*

## I. The Noise Was Always There

The first time you hear *The Money Store*, something happens that you do not expect from a hip-hop record: the album announces itself as an assault. Not a lyrical assault — hip-hop has always done that — but a *textural* one. The production on "Get Got" sounds like someone fed an 808 through a wood chipper. Zach Hill's drums don't sit in a mix so much as detonate inside it. And then MC Ride arrives, not rapping in any conventional sense but performing a kind of controlled vocal demolition, his voice compressed and distorted until it occupies the same frequency range as the noise around it. He becomes indistinguishable from the instrumentation.

This is 2012. The album is released on Epic Records — a major label — and it charts. It sells. It generates think pieces. And the critical consensus coalesces around a narrative that goes something like this: Death Grips fused hip-hop with industrial music and punk, creating something unprecedented.

The narrative is wrong. Not entirely, but in a way that matters.

Because the noise was always there. It was there in 1987, when the Bomb Squad stacked twenty to thirty sample layers on top of each other for Public Enemy's *It Takes a Nation of Millions to Hold Us Back*, deliberately overloading the E-mu SP-1200's 12-bit resolution until the compression clipped and distorted, producing what Hank Shocklee described as a kind of sonic collage — "almost like a Romare Bearden kind of painting." The Bomb Squad did not stumble into noise. They engineered it. They used half-inserted audio jacks to generate harmonic artifacts and kept the results as textural enhancements. They played records at the wrong speed to induce pitch distortion. They rejected reverb and low-end polish in favor of raw urgency. This was, by any honest definition, industrial production technique applied to Black music, years before anyone thought to call it that.

It was there earlier still. The scratch — the foundational sonic innovation of hip-hop — is a noise technique. Grand Wizard Theodore's accidental discovery in 1977 was the discovery that a vinyl record, played incorrectly, produces a sound that is musically useful precisely because it is not music in any conventional sense. It is the sound of the medium itself being abused. The scratch is hip-hop's original noise gesture, and it predates Throbbing Gristle's *D.o.A: The Third and Final Report* by a single year.

And the 808 — the Roland TR-808 drum machine that became hip-hop's signature instrument — was a commercial failure when Roland released it in 1980. It was supposed to sound like real drums. It did not. It sounded like an electronic approximation of drums, which is to say it sounded wrong, which is to say it sounded like noise. Hip-hop producers, particularly in the South, heard the wrongness and recognized it as the point. The 808's kick drum, pushed past its intended parameters, produces a sub-bass tone that is felt more than heard — a drone frequency that would be right at home on a Sunn O))) record.

So the noise was always there. This is the first thing to establish, and it is not trivial. It is the foundation of the argument.

But here is the second thing, which is equally important: what happened between 2010 and 2023 was not simply a continuation. It was an intensification, a deepening, and — crucially — a set of *deliberate choices* made by specific artists who understood exactly what they were doing and why. The noise tradition within hip-hop existed as a kind of latent potential, a set of techniques and sensibilities that had been present since the genre's founding but had never been pushed to their logical extremes within the frame of rap music. What changed in the 2010s was that a critical mass of artists decided to push.

This essay is about that push. It is about who pushed, how they pushed, and what got revealed in the pushing.

What got revealed is something uncomfortable, something that extends well beyond music criticism into the territory of racial epistemology — the study of how race shapes what counts as knowledge, and in this case, what counts as *music*. Because the boundary between "music" and "noise" has never been a purely acoustic phenomenon. It has always been a cultural construction, and in the West, that construction has always run along racial lines. When Throbbing Gristle or Einsturzende Neubauten made noise, it was filed under "art." When Memphis rappers made noise — and they made *plenty* — it was filed under "lo-fi," a term that implies deficiency rather than intent. The assumption was that the distortion on a Three 6 Mafia cassette was the result of poverty, not aesthetics. That the degradation was accidental, not compositional.

The 2010s convergence — the moment when industrial, noise, and grunge aesthetics visibly entered hip-hop's mainstream discourse — was both a genuine musical evolution and a perceptual correction. It was the moment when the critical apparatus was finally forced to reckon with the fact that hip-hop had been generating sonic extremity on its own terms, through its own internal lineages, for decades. And it happened, paradoxically, only when enough artists arrived who could code-switch between the languages of noise music and hip-hop — artists with art-school educations and punk-show histories who could translate one tradition into the vocabulary of the other.

This is the double helix of the story. One strand runs inside hip-hop: Memphis tape culture, chopped-and-screwed, phonk, the Southern noise tradition that generated its own extremity without ever needing to reference Throbbing Gristle. The other strand runs outside: industrial music, power electronics, post-punk, the UK bass continuum, the experimental underground that gradually converged on hip-hop's rhythmic structures. The two strands wound around each other throughout the 2010s, producing something genuinely new — not because the elements were new, but because their combination was, and because the artists doing the combining understood both traditions deeply enough to make the fusion feel inevitable rather than forced.

The stakes here are not small. This is ultimately a question about who gets to define what music is, who gets credit for innovation, and how the racial politics of listening shape the history we tell about sound.

## II. Hip-Hop's Own Noise

Before we can talk about what came from outside, we have to talk about what was already inside. And to talk about what was already inside, we have to go to Memphis.

In the early 1990s, something was happening in Memphis, Tennessee that had no real parallel elsewhere in hip-hop. A loose network of producers and rappers — DJ Paul and Juicy J (before they were Three 6 Mafia), Tommy Wright III, Blackout, Skinny Pimp, Playa Fly, the Graveyard Productions crew — were making music that sounded like nothing else in the genre. The beats were minimal and hypnotic: looped horror-movie samples, minor-key keyboard figures, and drum machine patterns built on the Roland TR-808 and its variants. The lyrics were violent, often hallucinatory, steeped in occult imagery and street nihilism. And the recordings themselves were lo-fi to a degree that would have been considered unacceptable anywhere else in commercial hip-hop.

This last point is critical. The lo-fi quality of early Memphis rap was not incidental. It was constitutive. Tommy Wright III began producing music using four-track recording equipment, dubbing tapes in his home and selling them out of car trunks and corner stores. DJ Paul and Juicy J's early releases — the pre-Three 6 Mafia tapes like *Volume 1* and the Prophet Posse material — were recorded on consumer-grade equipment and distributed on cassette. The format shaped the sound. Tape hiss became texture. Compression artifacts became rhythmic elements. The degradation of the medium became, through repetition and iteration, an aesthetic in its own right.

This is not a phenomenon unique to Memphis. It has exact parallels in Jamaican dub reggae, where Lee "Scratch" Perry and King Tubby turned the limitations of their equipment — broken spring reverbs, overloaded mixing boards, tape machines pushed past their tolerances — into a production philosophy that treated the studio itself as an instrument. In both cases, the poverty of the means became the richness of the aesthetic. What starts as constraint becomes style. What starts as degradation becomes composition.

But here is what makes Memphis unique within hip-hop: the darkness was *chosen*. Three 6 Mafia's *Mystic Stylez* (1995) did not sound the way it did solely because of equipment limitations. DJ Paul was deliberately sampling horror film scores — *The Return of the Living Dead*, *A Nightmare on Elm Street*, *Friday the 13th* — and building beats around their most unsettling moments. The vocal delivery was deliberately eerie: slurred, double-tracked, often drenched in effects that made the rappers sound like they were broadcasting from another dimension. The aesthetic was not the byproduct of poverty. It was the product of artistic vision that happened to be executed with limited resources.

This distinction matters enormously for the argument of this essay. If Memphis rap's noise elements were merely the result of economic deprivation, then they don't constitute a genuine noise tradition within hip-hop — they're just what happens when poor people make music. But if they were deliberate — if the lo-fi textures, the horror samples, the droning repetition, the hallucinatory atmosphere were chosen and cultivated as an aesthetic — then they represent an independent noise lineage within hip-hop itself, one that owes nothing to industrial music and everything to the specific conditions of Black life in the American South.

The evidence overwhelmingly supports the latter interpretation. The aesthetic persisted even as the resources improved. DJ Paul and Juicy J did not abandon the darkness when they got major-label money. *Mystic Stylez* was already a choice, not a compromise.

Three hundred miles west of Memphis, in Houston, Texas, another internal noise tradition was developing. DJ Screw — Robert Earl Davis Jr. — began his signature technique in 1990: slowing records down to 60-70 beats per minute, chopping them with turntable manipulations, and layering the results into extended mixes that could run for an hour or more. The chopped-and-screwed technique is, functionally, a drone technique. It takes existing music and stretches it until the melodies warp into something unrecognizable, until the vocals drop into inhuman registers, until the bass frequencies expand into a kind of sustained low-end hum that vibrates the body rather than the ear.

This is drone music. It is noise music. It is psychedelic music. It is all of these things simultaneously, and it was invented by a Black DJ in Houston in the early 1990s with no reference whatsoever to the European or American noise traditions. DJ Screw was not listening to La Monte Young. He was not studying the minimalist tradition. He was, by his own account and the account of those around him, making music that sounded the way the world sounded when you were leaning on codeine — syrupy, distorted, time-dilated, heavy. The technique was pharmacological phenomenology rendered as sound.

The critical establishment largely failed to recognize chopped-and-screwed as a noise tradition. The technique was filed under "regional variant" or "subgenre" rather than understood as what it actually was: a radical reimagining of the relationship between recorded sound and temporal experience, achieved through the systematic abuse of playback technology. If a white European had invented the same technique in an art-school context, it would have been immediately recognized as a contribution to the experimental music canon. Coming from Houston's Third Ward, it was coded as drug music.

These two lineages — Memphis horror-rap and Houston screw — converged in the late 2000s and early 2010s through a series of artists who understood both traditions and pushed them further.

The most important of these bridge figures is Lil Ugly Mane. Travis Miller — a Virginia-based artist with a background in punk and noise bands — released *Mista Thug Isolation* on Bandcamp in February 2012. The album is a Memphis rap record. Its beats are directly descended from DJ Paul's early production: looped, dark, built on 808 patterns and horror-movie atmosphere. Miller's flow channels the slurred menace of early Three 6 Mafia and Tommy Wright III. But the album is also something else. Miller's background in extreme music — black metal, noise, punk — inflected his production with a density and textural complexity that went beyond his Memphis sources. *Mista Thug Isolation* is what happens when someone who has internalized the aesthetics of both traditions makes a record that refuses to choose between them. The lo-fi quality is deliberate. The horror is genuine. And the noise elements — the distortion, the tape saturation, the droning bass — are simultaneously Memphis techniques and noise-music techniques, because those are, as this essay argues, the same thing.

*Mista Thug Isolation* proved something. It proved that the gap between Memphis rap and noise music was not a gap at all but a shared sensibility viewed from different cultural positions. Miller could see across the gap because he had literally lived on both sides of it.

Around the same time, SpaceGhostPurrp was doing parallel work from Miami. He founded the Raider Klan collective in 2008, coined the term "phonk" to describe their sound, and released the now-legendary *Blackland Radio 66.6* mixtape in 2011. SpaceGhostPurrp's production was explicitly indebted to early Memphis rap — the looped samples, the lo-fi textures, the occult imagery — but filtered through a contemporary sensibility that emphasized atmosphere over aggression. The phonk revival that SpaceGhostPurrp initiated became one of the dominant underground aesthetics of the 2010s, and its influence reached unexpected places: A$AP Rocky's early work was directly shaped by his encounter with Raider Klan's sound, after A$AP Yams discovered SpaceGhostPurrp through Tumblr in late 2011.

The line from here to the present runs through a specific node: the producer collective Working on Dying and its co-founder F1lthy (Richard Ortiz). F1lthy's production for Playboi Carti — particularly on *Whole Lotta Red* (2020) — translated the Memphis-phonk lineage into something that would be called "rage" or "rage trap": distorted, aggressive, punk-inflected beats built on the same foundation of 808 abuse and textural extremity that DJ Paul had established three decades earlier. When Carti screams over F1lthy's blown-out production on *Whole Lotta Red*, he is participating in a noise tradition that runs back through SpaceGhostPurrp and Lil Ugly Mane to Memphis and Houston — a tradition entirely internal to hip-hop, entirely Black in its origins, and entirely independent of industrial music.

This is the first strand of the double helix. Hip-hop generated its own noise. It did not need Throbbing Gristle. It did not need Einsturzende Neubauten. It did not need Nine Inch Nails. The noise was always there.

## III. When Industrial Met Hip-Hop

And yet the other strand exists. It would be dishonest to pretend otherwise. Because while hip-hop was generating its own noise traditions in Memphis and Houston, a parallel set of artists — operating from the worlds of industrial music, post-punk, and the UK bass continuum — were converging on hip-hop's rhythmic structures from the outside. And their contributions were real, even if the credit politics around those contributions have been badly mishandled.

The earliest credible instance of industrial music explicitly engaging with hip-hop is Mark Stewart's *Learning to Cope with Cowardice* (1983), recorded with Adrian Sherwood's On-U Sound collective. Stewart, formerly of the Bristol post-punk band The Pop Group, made a record that fused dub production techniques with industrial noise and — crucially — the rhythmic sensibility of early hip-hop. The record is not a hip-hop record. But it is a record that understands hip-hop's rhythmic innovations and feeds them through a distinctly industrial processing chain. It is the first audible evidence of what would become a long and productive exchange.

This exchange developed most richly not in America but in the UK, and it is important to correct the Americocentric bias that dominates most tellings of this story. The UK continuum — a term borrowed from Simon Reynolds — produced a series of artists in the 1990s and 2000s who fused hip-hop rhythms with industrial textures in ways that were more sustained, more rigorous, and frankly more interesting than most of their American counterparts.

Techno Animal — the duo of Justin Broadrick (Godflesh, Napalm Death) and Kevin Martin — formed in 1990 and spent the next decade making records that merged dub production, dark ambient, and industrial noise with hip-hop beats. Their genre designation — "illbient," a term that fuses hip-hop's "ill" with ambient music — captures exactly the kind of cross-pollination they were attempting. Broadrick's background was in the heaviest music imaginable: he had been a teenager in Napalm Death and had founded Godflesh, one of the most important industrial metal bands in history. Martin's was in the experimental underground, traversing dub, jazzcore, and noise. Together, they made music that treated hip-hop's rhythmic foundation as a delivery mechanism for industrial-strength sonic density.

Mick Harris — Napalm Death's original drummer, the man credited with coining the term "grindcore" — took a different but parallel path with Scorn, founded in 1991. Scorn's evolution tracks the convergence in miniature: the early records are industrial metal, the later records are dub-inflected downtempo built on hip-hop beats and abyssal bass frequencies. Harris arrived at hip-hop through dub through metal, and the journey produced some of the most genuinely unsettling music of the 1990s.

Kevin Martin continued the project under his solo alias The Bug, making records that fused dancehall, grime, dub, and industrial noise into a sound that was, functionally, hip-hop's rhythmic DNA expressed through the production vocabulary of industrial music. The Bug's work is important for this essay because it demonstrates that the convergence between industrial and hip-hop was not unidirectional. It was not simply industrial artists borrowing hip-hop beats. It was a genuine meeting point — a shared interest in bass weight, rhythmic aggression, and the physical impact of sound — that could be reached from either direction.

The convergence was not limited to the Anglo-American axis. Japan's noise scene — Merzbow, the Boredoms, Masonna — had developed its own extreme sonic vocabulary through the 1980s and 1990s, one that would become a reference point for artists like JPEGMAFIA and the broader experimental hip-hop underground. The global reach of noise as a practice — from Tokyo to London to Lagos — suggests that the impulse toward sonic extremity is not culturally specific but the forms it takes are shaped by local conditions. What makes hip-hop's noise traditions distinct is not the noise itself but the rhythmic, lyrical, and cultural architecture through which that noise is organized.

On the American side, the most important early bridge was El-P. Jamie Meline's work with Company Flow — particularly *Funcrusher Plus* (1997) — established the template for what American underground hip-hop's engagement with industrial sound would look like. The production on *Funcrusher Plus* was described by *The New York Times* as "raunchy old funk snippets with electronic noise, making hip-hop that's simultaneously propulsive and disorienting." Pitchfork called it a "dusty-but-digital aesthetic" with a "beautiful-dystopia Blade Runner feel." El-P's subsequent Def Jux label became the home of an entire school of dystopian, noise-adjacent hip-hop: Cannibal Ox, Aesop Rock, Mr. Lif. But El-P's work, for all its innovation, remained recognizably hip-hop in its structure. The noise elements were additive — texture applied to conventional song forms. The beats were abrasive, but they were still *beats*.

The artists who went further — who genuinely dissolved the boundary between hip-hop and noise — were Dalek.

Dalek (pronounced "dialect") formed in Newark, New Jersey in 1998. The group — MC Dalek (Will Brooks) on vocals and production, Oktopus (Alap Momin) on electronics, and Joshua Booth on additional production — made music that was equally inspired by Public Enemy's dense collages, My Bloody Valentine's shoegaze walls, and Einsturzende Neubauten's industrial clatter. Their second album, *From Filthy Tongue of Gods and Griots* (2002), is the record that should occupy the position in this history that is typically assigned to Death Grips' *Exmilitary*. The title itself announces the project: "filthy tongue" — the abrasive, the confrontational, the noisy — in the service of "gods and griots" — the sacred tradition of the African oral historian who preserves and performs communal memory. Noise as preservation. Distortion as testimony.

*From Filthy Tongue of Gods and Griots* predates *Exmilitary* by nine years. It achieves a fusion of noise and hip-hop that is, in many respects, more complete and more radical than anything Death Grips would later attempt. Brooks' delivery — a resigned, despairing monotone delivering politically charged lyrics against walls of feedback and drone — anticipated MC Ride's approach by nearly a decade. And yet Dalek remains a footnote in most tellings of this story, while Death Grips occupies the center. The reasons for this disparity are worth examining honestly, and they are not flattering.

Then there is B L A C K I E — Michael LaCour, styled in all caps with spaces. A Houston noise-rap artist who had been performing his abrasive, confrontational music since the early 2000s, assembling his own sound systems rumored to exceed 5,000 watts and performing unscheduled shows in nightclubs and warehouses across Houston. B L A C K I E was making noise-rap before Death Grips existed. He was doing it in Houston, the city of DJ Screw, and he was explicit about the connection: "I wanna be like Bun B and DJ Screw," he told Vice. "Screw made Houston global. I wanna be an offshoot of that."

This is significant. B L A C K I E understood himself as working within Houston's internal tradition — the tradition of DJ Screw, of local innovation, of building your own infrastructure from nothing — while simultaneously making music that sounded like an industrial assault. He was the living proof that the two strands of the double helix were not as separate as the critical narrative suggested. And when Death Grips emerged in 2011 with a sound that bore unmistakable resemblance to what B L A C K I E had been doing for years, the credit politics became ugly. B L A C K I E claimed, with some justification, that Death Grips had been influenced by his work without acknowledgment. The claim has never been definitively settled, but its existence points to a structural problem in how credit is allocated in the music industry: the artist with the larger platform, the better-connected network, and — it must be said — the more legible art-school pedigree gets the credit, while the artist who was there first gets a Vice profile and a grudge.

The art-school dimension is worth addressing directly, because it is real and it matters. The artists who achieved the most visible fusions of hip-hop and noise in the 2010s were disproportionately products of higher education in the arts. MC Ride (Stefan Burnett) studied visual arts at Hampton University, a historically Black university in Virginia. Daveed Diggs graduated from Brown University with a degree in theatre arts in 2004. William Hutson holds a PhD in Theater and Performance Studies from UCLA; his dissertation, *Sonic Affects*, is a scholarly analysis of experimental electronic music and noise. Jonathan Snipes is a professional sound designer for the stage. Camae Ayewa — Moor Mother — holds a professorship in composition at USC's Thornton School of Music.

This is not an indictment. These are brilliant artists who made brilliant work, and their education contributed directly to the sophistication of what they produced. But it is a class dimension that deserves honest acknowledgment. The artists who successfully translated noise aesthetics into hip-hop were, overwhelmingly, artists who had been trained in the languages of both traditions — who could cite Macronympha and Memphis, who could reference power electronics and Public Enemy. The fusion required bilingualism, and bilingualism required access to institutions where both languages were spoken. The artists who had been doing the work without that institutional access — B L A C K I E in Houston, the Memphis producers who invented lo-fi noise-rap in the first place — remained marginal.

And then there is the Saul Williams collaboration. Williams — a poet, actor, and rapper whose work had always occupied the border between spoken word and hip-hop — released *The Inevitable Rise and Liberation of NiggyTardust!* in 2007, produced entirely by Trent Reznor. The title's reference to Bowie's Ziggy Stardust was deliberate: this was a concept album about reinvention, about the construction and destruction of identity, rendered through the fusion of Williams' confrontational lyricism with Reznor's industrial production. The album was released as a free or pay-what-you-want download — a distribution strategy that anticipated the approach Death Grips would later adopt with *Exmilitary*. It received a 4.5-star review from AllMusic, who called it "Williams' finest moment, and interestingly, one of Reznor's, too." Williams, like many of the artists in this story, came from the spoken-word poetry scene and the New York downtown performance world. The collaboration was possible because both artists spoke the same institutional language.

## IV. The Explosion: 2011-2016

By 2011, the two strands of the double helix had wound close enough together that a series of nearly simultaneous breakthroughs became possible. What followed, over the next five years, was the most radical sonic expansion hip-hop had undergone since the Bomb Squad era — a proliferation of artists, records, and approaches that collectively demonstrated that the boundary between hip-hop and noise was not just permeable but, for a growing cohort of artists, irrelevant.

Death Grips were the most visible of these breakthroughs, and they deserve their prominence even as they do not deserve their exclusivity. *Exmilitary*, released for free in April 2011, opens with "Beware" — a track that samples a Charles Manson interview and a Jane's Addiction guitar riff, layering them under MC Ride's declaration: "I close my eyes and seize it / I clench my fists and beat it / I light my torch and burn it / I am the beast I worship." The thesis is stated in the first minutes: this is music about self-annihilation as liberation, about pushing past the threshold where sound becomes intolerable and finding, on the other side, something that feels like freedom.

"Guillotine," the track that introduced most listeners to Death Grips, is structured around a hook that is less a melody than a mechanism: "It goes, it goes, it goes, it goes / Guillotine, yah." The repetition mimics the inexorable drop of a blade. The production — built by Zach Hill and Andy Morin — is claustrophobic in the way that Hill himself described: anxiety-fueled, airless, a music designed to replicate the feeling of being trapped inside a body that is trying to destroy itself.

*The Money Store* (2012) brought this approach to a major label, and the fact that it worked — commercially, critically — changed the conversation. But the conversation it changed was primarily about *perception*. Death Grips did not invent the fusion of noise and hip-hop. Dalek had done it nine years earlier. B L A C K I E had done it in Houston. The Bomb Squad had done it, if we are being honest, in 1988. What Death Grips did was make the fusion *legible* to the indie-rock critical establishment — to Pitchfork, to the music blogs, to the festival circuit that booked them alongside Aphex Twin and Swans. They translated noise-rap into a language that the existing critical infrastructure could process, and they did it in part because their members — Hill from the Sacramento experimental scene, Morin from electronic music — had the right connections and the right pedigree to be taken seriously by that infrastructure.

This is not to diminish Death Grips. *The Money Store* is a great record. But it is to insist that the credit be distributed honestly, and to note that Dalek's *From Filthy Tongue of Gods and Griots* deserves co-credit for the territory that Death Grips are typically given sole ownership of.

Clipping arrived in 2013 with *midcity*, their debut album, and offered a version of the noise-rap fusion that was, in some ways, more rigorous than Death Grips'. The group had started in 2009 as a remix project: Hutson and Snipes would take a cappellas of mainstream rap artists and create power electronics and noise remixes of them. Diggs joined in 2010 to write original raps over their compositions. The resulting music was built on a specific conceptual premise: what if rap vocals — technically proficient, narrative-driven, rooted in hip-hop tradition — were placed on top of production drawn entirely from the noise and power-electronics traditions? Not hip-hop beats with noise elements added, but actual noise compositions treated as if they were beats.

The production methodology was concrete: Hutson and Snipes built their tracks from real-world samples — bottles being hit, bricks breaking, field recordings of urban environments — run through signal processing chains derived from the power electronics tradition. Snipes worked in Max/MSP, building custom instruments that generated textures no conventional production software could produce. Hutson brought his academic knowledge of noise music's history and theory — his UCLA dissertation had included a sustained analysis of Macronympha's treatment of deindustrialization and working-class affect. The result was hip-hop that sounded like it was being performed inside a collapsing building, and it was completely deliberate.

On "Body & Blood," the constraint produces a narrative that is simultaneously lurid and clinical: "She don't need you for shit but your dick and your veins / And your guts and your (body and blood)." The pronoun discipline -- "she," never "I" -- transforms what could be confessional horror into forensic observation. Diggs is not telling you how it felt. He is telling you what happened, and the distance between those two modes is where the dread lives.

Danny Brown's *Atrocity Exhibition* (2016) achieved something different: it brought the noise-rap sensibility to an audience that identified primarily as hip-hop listeners, not indie-rock fans or noise enthusiasts. The album's title is borrowed from a Joy Division song, itself borrowed from a J.G. Ballard novel — a lineage that runs from post-punk to postmodern literature to Detroit rap, and that captures the cross-pollination at the heart of this essay. The opening track, "Downward Spiral," details a three-day drug binge over production by Paul White that has been described as the sound of "a reanimated corpse punching his way out of a coffin." The drums clatter. The horns blare at wrong angles. The synths bleat like hallucinations.

What makes *Atrocity Exhibition* essential to this story is Brown's specificity. He is not making noise for noise's sake. He is using abrasive production as a correlative for the experience of addiction — the paranoia, the sensory overload, the feeling that reality itself has become hostile. On "Ain't It Funny," produced with a Jonah Hill-directed music video that frames Brown's self-destruction as a sitcom, the gap between the song's domestic framing and its sonic violence is the entire point. The noise is not decoration. It is the sound of a mind coming apart, and Brown's decision to express that coming-apart through industrial-inflected production rather than conventional hip-hop beats is a deliberate artistic choice rooted in the recognition that conventional beats cannot carry the weight of what he is trying to say.

The bars make the mechanism explicit: "Octopus in a straight jacket / Savage with bad habits / Broke serving fiends, got rich became a addict / Ain't it funny how it happens." The irony of "ain't it funny" is structural, not rhetorical -- the production lurches and clatters around Brown's voice like the room itself is coming apart, and the lyrics document the disintegration with a precision that makes the chaos legible without making it comfortable.

Ho99o9 (pronounced "horror") — the Newark, New Jersey duo of theOGM and Yeti Bones — came at the fusion from the punk side. Their 2017 debut *United States of Horror* was, as Spin put it, an act of "reclaiming Black rage." They were Black artists playing bills with mostly white bands to mostly white audiences, and they understood the politics of that position with a clarity that informed every aspect of their work. They drew explicitly from Bad Brains — the all-Black hardcore punk band that had faced the same paradox in the 1980s — and from the tradition of Black punk that the mainstream narrative of punk history had systematically erased. Ho99o9's fusion of hardcore punk and hip-hop was not a gimmick. It was a political argument about the ownership of sonic aggression, made in sound.

Moor Mother — Camae Ayewa — released *Fetish Bones* on Don Giovanni Records in 2016 and immediately established herself as one of the most important artists working at the intersection of noise, hip-hop, and Black radical tradition. Ayewa had grown up in public housing in Aberdeen, Maryland, moved to Philadelphia to study photography at the Art Institute, and spent years organizing and performing in Philadelphia's underground music community before her debut. *Fetish Bones* used noise as historical testimony — drone, feedback, distortion, and fragmented vocals deployed to convey the experience of Black life under conditions of structural violence. The noise was not metaphorical. It was the sound of history itself — the Middle Passage, the plantation, the prison, the housing project — compressed into frequencies that the body registers as physical pain.

On "Deadbeat Protest," Ayewa distills this into eighty-three seconds of pure confrontation: "Trying to save my black life / By fetishizing my dead life / Fuck, get away from me." The lines collapse the distance between the protest sign and the body bag, between solidarity and spectacle. And then, over a wall of static: "Did you see my dead body at the protest?" The question is not metaphorical. In the noise, the distinction between activism and autopsy dissolves.

Ratking — the New York trio of rappers Wiki and Hak and producer Sporting Life — released *So It Goes* in April 2014 and created the definitive document of noise-rap as urban geography. The album, engineered by Young Guru (Jay-Z's longtime engineer, a choice that grounded the project firmly in hip-hop tradition), sounded like New York City: cluttered, aggressive, built on layers of vocal samples chopped beyond recognition, robotic hi-hats, and stray sounds that seemed to leak in from the street outside the studio. *The New York Times* ranked it among the best albums of 2014. Vice called it the best. It was hip-hop that owed as much to punk as to rap, and it was specifically *New York* in a way that connected it to the long history of the city's experimental traditions — from no wave to hip-hop to noise.

And beneath all of these individual breakthroughs, a structural shift was underway that made the explosion possible: the democratization of production technology. Between 2010 and 2013, the cost of professional-quality music production collapsed. DAWs became affordable or free. Bandcamp launched in 2008 and by 2012 had become the primary distribution platform for independent and experimental music. SoundCloud, founded in 2007, enabled a generation of producers to share work without any intermediary. The bedroom producer — a figure that had existed since the four-track era but had been constrained by equipment costs and distribution barriers — became the dominant mode of music creation in the underground.

This matters because the noise-rap explosion was, in large part, a *bedroom* phenomenon. Lil Ugly Mane made *Mista Thug Isolation* at home and released it on Bandcamp. SpaceGhostPurrp distributed Raider Klan material through Mediafire and Tumblr. Death Grips released *Exmilitary* for free online. The technology did not cause the aesthetic shift — the aesthetic impulses were already present in the traditions described above — but it removed the gatekeepers who would have prevented that shift from reaching an audience.

The result was a market correction: the technology caught up with the aesthetic, and the audience that had always existed for this music -- scattered across forums, blogs, and recommendation algorithms -- was finally able to find it. By 2016, the boundary between hip-hop and noise had been crossed so many times that it had ceased to exist as a meaningful category. What replaced it was a spectrum of sonic aggression that ran from Playboi Carti's distorted trap to Moor Mother's noise poetry, with Death Grips, clipping., Danny Brown, and dozens of other artists along the way.

---

## V. The Consolidation: 2016-2023, From Underground to Institution

The question was no longer whether hip-hop could absorb noise aesthetics. It had. The question became what infrastructure would sustain the artists doing this work, and what would happen when the underground's radical experiments started winning prizes.

The answer begins in Baltimore, in a row house that doubled as a studio, with a producer-rapper who named his debut album after the only Black Republican running for president.

JPEGMAFIA's *Black Ben Carson* (2016) was a provocation engineered with forensic precision. The title alone accomplished what most political rap albums spend fourteen tracks attempting: it forced the listener to hold two contradictory ideas simultaneously. Ben Carson, the neurosurgeon-turned-politician, was both a genuine achievement of Black excellence and a figure whose political alignment made him, in the eyes of much of Black America, a paradox at best and a betrayal at worst. JPEGMAFIA did not resolve this tension. He inhabited it.

What made *Black Ben Carson* more than a clever title was the production. JPEGMAFIA produced the entire album himself, working across multiple laptops and DAWs, building a methodology that treated the recording process itself as an instrument. By the time *Veteran* arrived in January 2018, that methodology had crystallized into something genuinely new. The album opens with "1539 N. Calvert" -- named for the address of the Baltimore Bell Foundry, the shuttered DIY arts space that had been a nerve center for the city's noise and experimental scenes. The title is a tribute and a eulogy. The production is neither: it is alive, hostile, and unpredictable, chopping samples into shards and rebuilding them into structures that feel simultaneously like hip-hop and like nothing hip-hop had hosted before.

JPEGMAFIA's politics were not decorative. They were load-bearing. He titled a track "I Might Vote 4 Donald Trump" not as endorsement but as what he later described as "a protest song about the entire election... commentary on how ridiculous and reality tv American politics has become." This was an artist who understood that provocation without craft is trolling, but provocation married to genuine musical innovation is a tradition that runs from Chuck D through Boots Riley through Peggy himself.

On "Baby I'm Bleeding," the provocation is inseparable from the craft: "When it comes to money, bet these niggas is a no-show" lands over production that sounds like a sample being dragged across asphalt, and the opening lifts Edge's WWE entrance theme -- "you think you know me" -- recontextualized from arena spectacle into something genuinely menacing. The borrowing is the argument: Peggy treats all of American culture as raw material, and the abrasion is what happens when you process it honestly.

*Veteran* landed on year-end lists across the critical establishment. JPEGMAFIA went from Baltimore basements to festival stages. The infrastructure that enabled this -- Bandcamp for direct sales, social media for audience-building without label gatekeeping, the sheer cheapness of laptop production -- was the same infrastructure available to every artist in this lineage. What distinguished Peggy was the refusal to smooth any edges for the sake of accessibility. The noise was not a phase to be outgrown. It was the architecture.

---

On the opposite end of the temperamental spectrum -- though not of the sonic one -- billy woods and ELUCID had been building something quieter and more durable.

Armand Hammer, the duo's collaborative project, released *Race Music* in 2013 and *Rome* in 2017, but it was the run beginning with *Paraffin* (2018) and extending through *Haram* (2021, produced by the Alchemist) and *We Buy Diabetic Test Strips* (2023) that established them as the most consistently excellent act in underground hip-hop. The key to understanding Armand Hammer is understanding that the abrasion in their music is not an aesthetic choice layered on top of conventional rap. It is structural. ELUCID's production work -- dense, sample-heavy, built from dissonant textures and unexpected sonic collisions -- creates environments that billy woods' lyrics inhabit the way a body inhabits weather. You do not choose your relationship to it. You endure it, or you engage with it, but you do not ignore it.

On "Asylum," the opening track of *Aethiopes*, woods drops listeners into a world without orientation: "Razor wire like a Slinky / Rumor is parcel bomb took the secretary right eye and pinky." The simile -- razor wire as children's toy -- compresses violence and innocence into a single image, and the specificity of "right eye and pinky" refuses the comfort of abstraction. Elsewhere: "Spare me the Hallmark Karl Marx / I was in the Dollar Tree breakroom playing cards with quarters." This is what density sounds like in practice -- two bars that dismiss performative radicalism while grounding class consciousness in the specific, unglamorous texture of minimum-wage labor.

billy woods' contribution to this lineage extends beyond his own discography. Backwoodz Studioz, the label he founded in 2002, became one of the most important independent infrastructure projects in twenty-first-century hip-hop. The label's roster -- including ELUCID, ShrapKnel, and a growing constellation of artists operating at the intersection of experimental production and dense lyricism -- represented a model: the label as ecosystem, not just distribution channel. Backwoodz demonstrated that you could build a sustainable operation serving a dedicated audience without ever courting mainstream attention.

What Backwoodz shared with Deathbomb Arc -- the LA-based label that had released early Death Grips material and signed clipping. -- was a philosophy about what a label could be. William Hutson of clipping. described Deathbomb's ethos precisely: "The modus operandi of Deathbomb is punk as a way of being in the world, and not a type of music." This distinction matters enormously. These were not noise-rap labels. They were labels that understood independence as a practice rather than a genre designation, and whose rosters happened to include artists making music that sounded like what independence actually feels like when it refuses to compromise.

---

Injury Reserve's *By the Time I Get to Phoenix* (2021) is the album in this lineage that most resists critical framing, because its central fact is not sonic but biographical. Stepa J. Groggs died on June 29, 2020, at age thirty-two, while the album was being recorded. The remaining members -- Ritchie with a T and producer Parker Corey -- completed the record after a two-month hiatus. The album is dedicated to Groggs.

What this means sonically is that *By the Time I Get to Phoenix* sounds like grief processed through the only tools its makers had: experimental production, fractured vocal performances, and a willingness to let the music fall apart when the emotions demanded it. The album opens with "Outside," a track of building, breathless, pounding energy that feels less like a song and more like a panic attack rendered in audio. "Superman That" follows with discombobulated percussion and vocoder processing that strips the human voice of its humanity -- not as a stylistic choice but as an honest representation of what loss does to communication. The group had worked with Black Midi's Morgan Simpson on the production, and that collaboration pushed the sound into territory where the distinction between noise, post-punk, and hip-hop became genuinely irrelevant.

The album's title references Isaac Hayes, but its emotional logic references the experience of trying to reach someone who is no longer there. The noise in *By the Time I Get to Phoenix* is not confrontational in the way Death Grips or JPEGMAFIA can be confrontational. It is the noise of systems breaking down. Of signal degrading. Of trying to maintain coherence when the ground has shifted beneath you. If Death Grips made noise that sounded like rage, Injury Reserve made noise that sounded like the moment after rage, when what remains is confusion and the slow work of reassembling a self.

---

Backxwash's *God Has Nothing to Do with This Leave Him Out of It* won the 2020 Polaris Music Prize, and the fact that this sentence can be written at all represents a seismic shift in what institutional recognition was willing to embrace.

Ashanti Mutinta, a trans woman from Zambia living in Montreal, built the album in FL Studio, sampling Black Sabbath and Led Zeppelin and David Lynch's *Eraserhead*, constructing a sonic world where metal's theatricality met hip-hop's directness met the visceral urgency of a person whose existence was contested on multiple axes simultaneously. The album opens with Ozzy Osbourne's voice howling from "Black Sabbath" -- the same sample other producers had used for decades, but where earlier producers had sampled it for the groove, Backxwash heard something different. She heard the fear. The anguish.

"I see samples more than just sounds," she explained in an NPR interview. "They represent a slice of time." This is a deceptively profound statement about production methodology. A sample is not just an audio fragment. It is a cultural artifact carrying the full weight of its original context, and the act of recontextualizing it is an act of interpretation. When Backxwash samples Ozzy Osbourne, she is not borrowing metal's sonic vocabulary. She is claiming it. She is asserting that the terror in Osbourne's voice -- the theatrical, performative terror of a white British rock band playing with satanic imagery -- maps onto something real in her experience. The performance becomes testimony.

The lyrics make the stakes explicit. On the title track: "Mama keep telling me to ask the lord for forgiveness / I want war with these bitches." On "Spells": "I told my mama that the devil got a place for me / I'm going to hell." The mother-daughter relationship, the invocation of damnation, the refusal of the forgiveness narrative -- these are not metaphors dressed in metal production. They are dispatches from a life where the noise of spiritual condemnation and the noise of the music are the same frequency.

After winning the Polaris, she told CBC Music: "My existence itself is political, my livelihood is political, and the livelihood of my sisters is political." This was not rhetoric. It was a description of material conditions. In Zambia, homosexuality carries a prison sentence. In Canada, trans women of color face violence at rates that the statistics struggle to capture. The noise in Backxwash's music is not metaphorical. It is the sound of existing in conditions that are themselves noisy -- contradictory, dangerous, unresolved.

---

Meanwhile, in New York, Show Me the Body and their Corpus collective were building something that looked less like a label and more like a social movement with a Bandcamp page.

Founded in 2009, Show Me the Body -- led by banjoist and vocalist Julian Cashwan Pratt -- played their earliest shows under bridge overpasses, in alleys, in basements. Their music combined post-hardcore, sludge metal, noise, and hip-hop into something that refused genre categorization not as an aesthetic position but as a practical consequence of the musicians involved and the scenes they moved through. The Corpus collective expanded this into a community infrastructure project. *Corpus I* (2017) featured Princess Nokia, Cities Aviv, Moor Mother, Eartheater, and Negashi Armada -- a roster that made sense only if you understood the collective not as a genre exercise but as a network of artists united by methodology and mutual aid.

What connected all of these projects -- Backwoodz, Deathbomb Arc, Corpus, the loose network of Bandcamp-native labels serving this ecosystem -- was a shared understanding that the music could not be separated from its infrastructure. The noise was not just sonic. It was organizational. These artists were building alternatives to the systems that had historically determined which music reached which ears, and the abrasion in their sound was, at least in part, a reflection of the friction involved in building those alternatives.

---

## VI. The Production Revolution: Tools, Techniques, and the Democratization of Noise

The story told so far -- of artists making deliberate creative choices, building infrastructure, engaging in dialogue with music history -- cannot be fully understood without accounting for the material conditions that made those choices possible. Between 2010 and 2013, the economics of music production underwent a transformation so fundamental that it is not an exaggeration to call it a revolution. And like most revolutions, it was not driven by ideology but by price.

In December 2011, Apple dropped the price of Logic Pro from $499 to $199. FL Studio had long offered lifetime free updates with purchase, meaning a one-time investment of $99-$199 gave a producer permanent access to a professional-grade DAW. Ableton began bundling Lite versions free with audio interfaces that themselves cost under $100. By 2013, the full professional signal chain -- DAW, interface, monitors, microphone -- could be assembled for under $500. A decade earlier, the equivalent setup would have cost $5,000 to $10,000 and required physical studio space.

This matters because noise, by its nature, is production-intensive. You cannot make a Death Grips record or a clipping. record with just a microphone and a beat from the internet. The democratization of production tools did not cause the noise-rap explosion -- the artistic impulses predate the price drops -- but it removed the economic barrier that had previously limited this kind of work to artists with either label budgets or institutional resources.

---

The specific production methodologies that emerged are worth examining in detail, because they reveal how the tools shaped the sound and vice versa.

El-P's approach, developed in the late 1990s and refined across the next two decades, begins with the Ensoniq EPS-16+, a sampling keyboard whose variable sampling rate (11.2 to 44.1 kHz) became the foundation of his sonic signature. "Almost all of his samples go in at 22.3 kHz," as he explained in a foundational interview, "because it just gives it that little edge." That edge -- the subtle degradation introduced by sampling below full resolution -- is the key to El-P's sound. He compounds it by sampling at deliberately high input levels, introducing distortion at the point of capture rather than adding it later as an effect. The distortion is baked into the raw material. It is not applied; it is constitutive.

clipping. took a different approach entirely. Jonathan Snipes and William Hutson, both trained in experimental and noise music traditions, built their production from musique concrete principles -- the mid-twentieth-century practice of constructing music from recorded environmental sounds rather than traditional instruments. For "Work Work," they smashed cinder blocks, recorded the impacts, played them backward and forward to construct snare drums. Snipes mangled a beer can close to microphones, captured the harsh pops, and applied delay to create hi-hat patterns. He recorded hundreds of hits, scrapes, and flicks on a metal thermos, cut those recordings into sampler instruments, and sequenced them with MIDI. The result sounds like a DJ Mustard beat from a parallel universe where all instruments have been replaced by construction-site debris. This is not abstraction for abstraction's sake. It is a rigorous methodology: start with the sonic vocabulary of west coast club rap, then rebuild every element from non-musical sources, testing whether the groove survives the translation.

Daveed Diggs' contribution to clipping.'s methodology is equally systematic. He writes all his raps in second or third person -- "all 'I, me' language is off limits." This constraint, maintained across four full-length albums, is not a gimmick. It is a structural decision about the relationship between rapper and narrative. By refusing first-person address, Diggs transforms rap from testimony into reportage, from confession into observation. The effect, combined with the noise-based production, is music that feels simultaneously intimate and clinical -- you are close to the events described but never inside the consciousness experiencing them. It is hip-hop as documentary, shot on broken equipment.

JPEGMAFIA's methodology is the most protean and the hardest to pin down, because it changes with each project. For *Scaring the Hoes* (2023), his collaboration with Danny Brown, he imposed a constraint inspired by Madlib's approach to *Madvillainy*: every beat was made using a single Roland SP-404 sampler. "I used one machine and charted," he later noted. "These people have 18 people tweaking hi-hats and can't feed their kids." The statement is characteristically pugnacious, but the underlying argument is serious: constraint produces creativity, and the fetishization of expensive equipment is, for Peggy, a form of ideological capture. If you can make a charting album on a single sampler, the apparatus of modern production -- the armies of co-producers, the racks of boutique synthesizers, the pristine studio environments -- is revealed as optional. The noise in JPEGMAFIA's music is partly a consequence of this philosophy: when you work fast, on limited equipment, with an aesthetic that values rawness over polish, the result carries the artifacts of its own making.

Backxwash's FL Studio workflow represents yet another model: the bedroom producer as auteur. Working alone, sampling metal records and horror film soundtracks, she built complete sonic worlds in a DAW that costs less than a pair of studio headphones. The democratic implications are significant. A trans woman from Zambia, living in Montreal, with no label support and no studio access beyond her laptop, made an album that won the most prestigious music prize in Canada. The technology did not create the art. But the technology's accessibility removed the gatekeepers who would have prevented it.

---

A production element that cuts across all of these artists is vocal processing -- or, more precisely, the deliberate choice about how much processing to apply.

MC Ride's vocals in Death Grips are largely raw: shouted, screamed, delivered with a physicality that the microphone captures rather than shapes. The minimal processing is itself a production decision -- it places Ride's body in the mix as an unmediated presence, a human organism generating sound through exertion. JPEGMAFIA, by contrast, stacks and layers his vocals, creating densities that blur the line between voice and instrument. Danny Brown's natural vocal range -- that distinctive nasal register that can shift from conversational to manic within a single bar -- requires no processing to achieve its unsettling effect; the instrument itself is already "noisy." Playboi Carti's vocal approach on *Whole Lotta Red* achieves its distorted quality not through studio processing but through the physical act of recording at extreme volume -- the organic rage captured by a microphone pushed past its comfort zone.

Each of these approaches maintains what makes the music hip-hop rather than industrial or noise in the traditional sense. The voice raps in bars. The beat functions as a grid. The kick drum and the vocal remain centered in the mix. Samples, when used, reference hip-hop's own archive. The noise is incorporated into hip-hop's architecture; it does not replace that architecture. This is the critical distinction that many commentators miss: these artists are not making industrial music with rapping on top. They are making hip-hop that has absorbed noise as a vocabulary while retaining hip-hop's structural DNA.

---

Two platforms deserve specific attention for their role in this ecosystem.

Bandcamp served a crucial function: it provided infrastructure for economic survival. For artists working with uncleared samples -- which is to say, for nearly every artist in this lineage -- Bandcamp's pay-what-you-want model and direct-to-artist payment structure created a space where the legal ambiguity of sample-based production was less immediately threatening than it would be on a major streaming platform. billy woods released albums on Bandcamp for years before achieving broader critical recognition. JPEGMAFIA's *Veteran* was initially a Bandcamp release. The platform did not just distribute this music; it enabled its existence by providing an economic model compatible with the legal gray zones in which sample-heavy experimental production necessarily operates.

SoundCloud's role was complementary: its audio compression introduced artifacts that became, for a generation of listeners, inseparable from the music itself. The platform's limitations became a texture.

## VII. The Cultural Architecture: Who Listens, Who Benefits, Who Gets Credit

Everything discussed so far -- the sonic genealogy, the production innovations, the infrastructure -- exists within a cultural context that this essay has an obligation to examine honestly. That context involves race, class, gender, and the politics of attention. It is the most difficult section to write, because it requires holding multiple truths simultaneously without collapsing them into a single narrative.

---

The "genre collapse" narrative of the 2010s is not wrong, exactly. But it is incomplete. Genre collapse was class-specific -- it described the listening habits of a particular demographic: young, internet-literate, culturally omnivorous, and drawn from backgrounds that provided the cultural capital to treat genre-hopping as sophistication rather than confusion.

JPEGMAFIA addressed this directly and without sentimentality. When confronted with the observation that his audience was predominantly white, he responded: "I get this question a lot. Like, 'Oh, you have a mostly white fan base.'" His answer reframed the question entirely: "Who does not have a mostly white fan base?" He elaborated: "If you're, as a black person, not okay with performing to mostly white people for your career, you should pick a different career because there's not enough n----s to even support that shit." This is not resignation. It is demographic realism. In a country where white people constitute the majority of the population and an even larger majority of the concert-going, vinyl-buying, Bandcamp-supporting audience for underground music, the racial composition of any underground artist's fan base will reflect that reality.

But acknowledging demographic reality does not resolve the ethical questions it raises. It sharpens them.

---

Consider the asymmetry in canonization. Death Grips, formed in 2010, were canonized almost immediately. *Exmilitary* (2011) and *The Money Store* (2012) were treated by the critical establishment as epochal, genre-defining works. The speed of consensus was remarkable. Within two years of their debut, Death Grips were widely understood as one of the most important musical projects of the decade.

Dalek debuted in 1998 with *Negro Necro Nekros*, an album that anticipated much of what Death Grips would later be celebrated for: industrial-weight production, dense atmospherics, political fury rendered in noise. They built a bigger following in the UK and Europe than in the United States. Their critical recognition in America came slowly, grudgingly, and never approached the scale afforded to Death Grips. Moor Mother's *Fetish Bones* (2016) -- an album of extraordinary power -- received respectful reviews but nothing approaching the breathless canonization that greeted *The Money Store*.

What explains the differential? The answer is uncomfortable but not complicated. Death Grips arrived when the indie-rock critical establishment was searching for hip-hop legible within indie-rock frameworks. Zach Hill was a known quantity in the noise and math-rock scenes. The group's visual aesthetic mapped onto punk and noise traditions in ways that made them parsable by critics whose primary references were Swans and Big Black rather than Public Enemy and the Bomb Squad.

Dalek and Moor Mother made music whose experimental genealogy ran through Black radical traditions -- free jazz, Afrofuturist philosophy, the Black Arts Movement -- that the predominantly white critical establishment understood less intimately. The music was no less radical. The critics were less equipped to hear it.

This is not a conspiracy. It is an epistemological problem. Critics hear through frameworks, and the frameworks available to the indie-rock establishment of the 2010s were better calibrated for certain kinds of radicalism than others. The result was structural advantage: artists whose experiments resonated with existing critical frameworks received faster recognition than those whose experiments required critics to build new frameworks entirely.

---

The consumption of Black pain is a thread that runs through this entire lineage, and it must be named.

Death Grips' music is, among many things, a sustained encounter with extremity -- with violence, paranoia, rage, and the dissolution of stable selfhood. MC Ride's performances are physically demanding in a way that the word "performance" almost fails to capture; they are acts of exertion that leave his body visibly depleted. The audience for this music -- predominantly young, white, and male -- consumed this extremity as aesthetic experience. They moshed. They screamed along. They made memes. They treated "I've seen footage" as a catchphrase.

None of this is inherently wrong. Art exists to be consumed, and the energy of a Death Grips show is genuinely transcendent in ways that deserve celebration rather than hand-wringing. But the pattern -- Black artists producing music of extreme emotional and physical intensity, consumed by white audiences for whom that intensity is an aesthetic thrill rather than a lived condition -- is a pattern with a history that extends far beyond hip-hop. It connects to minstrelsy, to the consumption of blues and jazz by white audiences, to the entire history of American popular music as a mechanism for transmuting Black experience into white entertainment.

JPEGMAFIA's incorporation of racial slurs directed at white people into his live performances was, among other things, a test of this dynamic. If your audience is predominantly white, and you call them slurs from the stage, and they cheer -- what has been revealed? One reading: that the audience is sophisticated enough to understand the provocation as art. Another reading: that white comfort with Black rage, when that rage is aestheticized and contained within the frame of a concert, is itself a form of consumption. JPEGMAFIA seemed to hold both readings simultaneously, which is perhaps the only honest position available.

The phrase "scaring the hoes" -- which became the title of JPEGMAFIA and Danny Brown's collaborative album -- operates in this same ambiguous space. It describes playing aggressively experimental music in contexts that expect conventional bangers, but it has also become cultural capital among young men who use their tolerance for abrasive music as a marker of discernment. The gendered language is not incidental. The framework positions women as arbiters of the conventional and men as explorers of the extreme -- a reproduction of dynamics that noise and industrial music have struggled with for decades.

---

Which makes the queer dimension of this lineage not just important but structurally essential.

Moor Mother forged her project in Philadelphia's noise, alternative poetry, and queer activist scenes. Her work with Irreversible Entanglements, the free jazz quintet that formed during a protest against police violence, represents a mode of artistic production in which queerness, Blackness, and radical politics are not separate identity categories to be intersected but a single integrated practice. Backxwash, as discussed, is a trans woman from Zambia whose music treats her transness not as a subject to be explored but as a condition that shapes everything -- the samples she chooses, the emotional register she occupies, the volume at which the music demands to be heard. Moodie Black, the noise-rap project led by K-Death (Chris Martinez), a trans woman of color, released *Lucas Acid* (2018) on Fake Four Inc., an album that brought "an aggressive edge to trans identity," as Out Magazine described it -- lyrics exploring paranoia, isolation, and the experience of coming to terms with gender identity in spaces that claimed radicalism but often reproduced the same exclusions as the mainstream.

These artists are not tokens in a diversity argument. They are, in many cases, the artists pushing the sound furthest. The reason is not mystical. It is material. When your existence is already contested -- when your body is already read as transgressive, when the noise of social hostility is already the ambient condition of your life -- the step from lived experience to sonic expression is shorter. The noise is not adopted. It is reported.

The queer dimension also complicates the consumption narrative. The audience for Moor Mother or Backxwash or Moodie Black includes significant queer and trans listenership. The DIY venues and radical spaces where this music circulates are often queer-organized and queer-maintained. The dynamic here is not straightforwardly "Black artists performing for white audiences" but something more complex: marginalized artists performing for audiences that are themselves marginalized, within infrastructure built by and for communities that the mainstream has not accommodated.

---

These scenes were networked -- through touring circuits, through Bandcamp and social media, through the festival ecosystem -- but the local specificities mattered. The Baltimore DIY scene that shaped JPEGMAFIA was not interchangeable with the Philadelphia radical arts community that shaped Moor Mother. The specific textures of post-industrial American urban life -- and the specific ways in which race, class, and institutional neglect shaped those textures -- were different. The music reflected those differences even as it participated in a broader shared project.

---

The political context cannot be reduced to backdrop. Occupy Wall Street (2011), Black Lives Matter (2013-2014), Ferguson (2014), the Trump presidency (2015-2020), the pandemic and George Floyd protests (2020) -- each created not just political conditions but affective conditions, emotional environments in which certain kinds of music became necessary rather than merely interesting.

As anxiety and depression became publicly speakable, music that *sounded* like anxiety -- fragmented, high-frequency, unstable, refusing resolution -- found an audience that heard not alienation but recognition. You do not need to understand the politics of JPEGMAFIA or Death Grips to feel that their music represents the experience of being alive in a period of accelerating instability. The sound itself communicates.

The critical establishment's relationship to this music reveals its own internal contradictions. Pitchfork, which had spent the 2000s as the primary gatekeeper of indie-rock taste, pivoted in the 2010s toward broader coverage of hip-hop, pop, and R&B. But the structural dynamics of music criticism -- who writes, who edits, who assigns, who reads -- did not transform as quickly as the coverage mandate. The result was a publication that covered noise-rap and experimental hip-hop with genuine enthusiasm but whose critical frameworks still carried the residue of the indie-rock epistemology from which they had emerged. Billy woods' *Maps* received a rare Pitchfork Best New Music designation in 2023 -- a recognition that was both overdue and, in its lateness, revealing about the pace at which critical institutions recalibrate.

---

## VIII. The Noise Endures

In December 2020, Playboi Carti released *Whole Lotta Red*, and the discourse broke.

The album was polarizing in a way that few major-label releases manage. Its cover paid homage to the late-1970s punk fanzine *Slash*. Its production -- heavy distortion, serrated synthesizers, chaotic energy -- drew on the aesthetic vocabulary that the underground artists discussed in this essay had spent a decade developing. Producer F1lthy's work on tracks like "Stop Breathing" and "On That Time" supplied what critics described as "adrenal punk energy." Carti's vocal approach -- recording at extreme volume, letting the microphone capture organic distortion rather than applying it digitally -- was a technique with clear antecedents in MC Ride's performances and JPEGMAFIA's recording philosophy.

The question *Whole Lotta Red* posed was the question that every underground-to-mainstream translation poses: did the substance travel with the sound?

Yeat, Ken Carson, Destroy Lonely, SoFaygo -- the generation of artists who followed Carti's pivot took the distortion and ran with it. By 2022-2023, "rage rap" had become a recognized subgenre with its own production conventions: looping synthesizers, heavy 808s, high-energy trap rhythms, and a general commitment to sonic aggression that would have been unthinkable in mainstream hip-hop a decade earlier. Ken Carson's *A Great Chaos* (2023) peaked at number 11 on the Billboard 200. The mainstream had absorbed the distortion.

But absorption is not the same as continuation. The pattern is familiar from other lineages: punk becomes new wave becomes pop-punk becomes Blink-182. The formal properties survive. The political and aesthetic commitments that generated those formal properties do not necessarily survive with them. When distortion becomes a Spotify playlist category -- when "dark" and "aggressive" become algorithmic recommendations rather than artistic choices made against the grain -- the relationship between sound and meaning changes fundamentally.

---

There is, however, a counter-narrative, and intellectual honesty requires giving it full weight.

Trap's evolution toward distortion was not simply a case of the mainstream co-opting the underground. It was a parallel development with a shared ancestor: Memphis.

Three 6 Mafia, DJ Paul, Juicy J, Lord Infamous, and the broader Memphis rap ecosystem of the early 1990s created the DNA of trap music. The eerie synths, the distorted 808s, the horror-film atmospherics, the triplet flows that Lord Infamous pioneered -- these fed into trap through one lineage and into the noise-rap underground through another (through Lil Ugly Mane, through SpaceGhostPurrp, through phonk). Memphis is the hidden common ancestor. When Carti distorts his vocals, he is drawing on a tradition that includes both MC Ride and Three 6 Mafia. When JPEGMAFIA layers harsh digital textures over trunk-rattling bass, he is drawing on a tradition that includes both industrial music and the lo-fi cassette culture of early Southern rap. The underground and the mainstream are not opposites in this story. They are branches of the same tree, separated by class, by geography, by institutional affiliation, but sharing roots that run through the specific post-industrial conditions of American music-making.

This does not mean the distinction between Carti and clipping. is meaningless. It means the distinction is about intention, methodology, and infrastructure rather than about sound alone. clipping. build their beats from smashed cinder blocks and crumpled beer cans, and Daveed Diggs writes every bar in second or third person, because these constraints are constitutive of the project's meaning. Carti distorts his vocals because distortion sounds hard and the energy is transcendent. Both are valid. Neither is reducible to the other.

---

The noise endures for reasons that are structural rather than fashionable.

First: the independent infrastructure built during the 2010s -- Backwoodz Studioz, Deathbomb Arc, Corpus, Bandcamp as a platform, the network of DIY venues and festival slots -- continues to function. It is not dependent on mainstream attention or label investment. It can sustain artists at a level that permits continued production even when the critical spotlight moves elsewhere. billy woods can release an album a year because his infrastructure is designed for exactly that cadence.

Second: the political conditions that created demand for this music have not resolved. They have intensified. The anxiety, the institutional instability, the sense of living in a period where the ground shifts faster than language can track -- these conditions persist. Music that sounds like those conditions continues to find an audience that recognizes itself in the sound.

Third: generational normalization. For listeners who came of age with *The Money Store* or *Veteran* or *By the Time I Get to Phoenix*, noise in hip-hop is not transgressive. It is part of the vocabulary. These listeners will make music of their own, and they will draw on the full range of what they heard growing up. The integration of noise aesthetics into hip-hop is, for this generation, simply a fact about what hip-hop is.

Fourth, and perhaps most importantly: the Afrofuturist intellectual framework that artists like Moor Mother, Backxwash, and the Black Quantum Futurism collective have built around this music provides it with a theoretical architecture that extends well beyond sonic aesthetics. This is not music in search of a justification. It is music with a philosophy -- a philosophy that connects the sonic extremity to longer histories of Black radical thought, to questions about technology and liberation, to the ongoing project of imagining futures that the present has not yet authorized.

---

There is a question this essay cannot fully answer, but it must honestly pose it.

When noise becomes the new normal -- when distortion is a playlist category, when "experimental" is a marketing tag, when the underground's sonic vocabulary becomes the mainstream's decorative vocabulary -- what happens next? The history of popular music suggests that the underground will move again, will find new territories of sonic extremity that the mainstream has not yet colonized, and the cycle will repeat. But cycles are not inevitable. They are contingent on material conditions: on the availability of tools, on the existence of infrastructure, on the willingness of artists to do the work of building something that does not yet have an audience.

The noise was always there. What changed, across the decade documented in this essay, was who was willing to hear it.

Hip-hop's internal noise traditions -- from Public Enemy's Bomb Squad to Dalek to Death Grips to JPEGMAFIA to Armand Hammer to Injury Reserve to Backxwash to Moor Mother -- prove that the genre was generating sonic extremity independently, on its own terms, for its own reasons, long before the "genre collapse" narrative made it legible to listeners trained in other traditions. The convergence of the 2010s was real: hip-hop and noise and industrial and punk did, genuinely, flow into each other in ways that produced music none of those traditions could have produced alone. But the convergence was also a perceptual correction -- a moment when listening cultures expanded enough to hear what had been happening all along.

The artists documented here made deliberate choices. They chose noise. They chose abrasion. They chose to work in modes that the market did not reward and the critical establishment was slow to recognize. They built infrastructure. They built communities. They made music that sounded like the world they inhabited -- not a representation of that world, but its sonic equivalent. The bars were dense. The production was uncompromising. The politics were embedded in the form, not just the content.

This is hip-hop doing what hip-hop has always done: building music from the wreckage of what came before, from the materials at hand, from the conditions that could not be escaped but could be transformed into sound. The noise endures because the conditions that produce it endure. And the artists -- the ones making the choices, writing the bars, building the beats from smashed cinder blocks and sampled Black Sabbath and SP-404 loops and the raw sound of a human voice pushed past its limits -- they endure too.

The noise was never the point. The noise was the proof that someone was still making something, in conditions designed to prevent exactly that. Listen again. Listen harder. The signal is in the noise.
