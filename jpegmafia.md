### JPEGMAFIA, the Long Loop, and the Album That Closes It

## I. The Frame Was The Point

The most-quoted moment on *All My Heroes Are Cornballs* is not a beat or a flip — it is a single line, sung by Helena Deland, on a song called "Free the Frail." The line is: *don't rely on the strength of my image.* It is the most exposed thing JPEGMAFIA has ever put on record. In its original 2019 context it reads as a direct address to the listener: *don't reduce me to my persona, please evaluate me as a writer.* In retrospect — read after *LP!*, after *Scaring the Hoes*, after *Vultures 1*, after *I Lay Down My Life For You*, after the dragging procession of Director's Cuts that has defined the years since — the line reads as something more painful and more accurate. It is a thesis statement that the artist himself has been unable to live by. He has built his career on the strength of his image. He has known this all along. The question that has been quietly shaping the work since *Veteran* is what he intends to do about it.

That question is no longer hypothetical. On April 30, 2026 — the date this essay is being completed — JPEGMAFIA has released *EXPERIMENTAL RAP*, twenty-five tracks long, on his own PEGGY imprint under exclusive license to AWAL. The album's existence had been speculated for weeks; a *Consequence* anticipated-albums preview floated the title, an Instagram Reel and YouTube Short carried the announcement, fan forums had circulated a May 21 date that turned out to be wrong by three weeks. As of this morning the iTunes catalog confirms the release: *EXPERIMENTAL RAP*, 25 tracks, Hip-Hop, JPEGMAFIA. The opening track is sixty seconds, untitled — listed in the metadata only as "Track 1." Track 2 is called "babygirl," runs two minutes and twenty-seven seconds, and is the only song on the album whose name has surfaced through to the public-facing metadata at the time of writing. The remaining twenty-three tracks read as "Track 3" through "Track 25," some under a minute, several past three. Whether those placeholder names are a metadata-rollout artifact or a deliberate authorial choice is the first question the album asks of its listener — and the question is itself a Peggy gesture, since both readings are coherent with the catalog and either one would be characteristic.

The title is the artwork. Hendricks has spent a decade either rejecting or being resigned to the descriptor *experimental rap*, a category whose contemporary usage runs from the rigorous (the Anti-Pop-to-clipping. lineage, the Dälek industrial-rap canon, the Saul Williams politicized-noise tradition) to the merely textural (rage scene drum design, SoundCloud distortion, anything with a weird snare). To title an album *EXPERIMENTAL RAP* in 2026, at the moment when, by every objective measure (Vultures, Arirang, the Flume EP, the Director's Cut economy, the BTS Jersey-club credit), Hendricks has become the most mainstream-adjacent artist the descriptor still applies to, is to claim the word for himself. It is the same structural move as *Veteran*: take a noun that has been used about you and make it the title that you use about yourself. *Veteran* was the discharge document turned into a thesis. *EXPERIMENTAL RAP* is the genre tag turned into a frame. The title is a *reclamation*, and one whose seriousness will be tested track by track across twenty-five tracks of audio that, as of this writing, has been available for exactly the length of one full listen.

This essay was, until this morning, an essay of anticipation. It is now an essay of context. It cannot evaluate what *EXPERIMENTAL RAP* sounds like — the master has been public for a matter of hours; reviews have not landed; the lyrical content of "babygirl" and the other twenty-four tracks will need their own listening windows; the discourse has not yet metabolized the fact that the album is real. What this essay can do is set out the long approach: how Hendricks got from Brooklyn to Alabama to a Japanese barracks to a Baltimore warehouse to a Kanye production credit to a BTS placement to a 25-track record on his own imprint titled with the word that has dogged him since 2018. The album is the document. This is the road that produced it.

This essay is about that road. It is about the sample on top of the sample, the loop on top of the loop. It is about the production method Hendricks has refined into a signature so consistent that it now functions as a critical limitation. It is about the tension that has been the engine of the work since 2016 and that, on April 30, 2026, has been put on the table in the most direct form he has yet attempted. And it is about what to listen for inside *EXPERIMENTAL RAP* — the album that is now the answer to the question "Free the Frail" asked seven years ago, and the album whose answer the next several months of listening will be required to read.

What the next record will sound like is a question. The more interesting question is what it cannot avoid being about.

## II. Brooklyn, Alabama, Japan: The Long Approach

The biography matters because the production matters, and the production was always biographical. Hendricks was born October 22, 1989, in Flatbush, Brooklyn, to Jamaican parents. He spent his early childhood there — the Brooklyn that is referenced repeatedly across his catalog, the Brooklyn whose gentrification "Williamsburg" eulogizes against a Travis Scott "Butterfly Effect" sample, the Brooklyn whose rap canon he has both submitted to and resisted. Around age twelve or thirteen the family moved south. The dominant version of the story, the one Hendricks has told in long-form interviews and to Talib Kweli on *People's Party*, names Alabama — though Wikipedia and a handful of secondary bios have been contagiously committed to "Louisiana," in what appears to be downstream of an under-cited revision. This essay follows Hendricks' own account: Alabama. The detail matters less than the substance, which is consistent across both versions: he encountered, in his words, "a lot of deep-rooted racism, head-on, at the age of twelve. No one helped me through it. A lot of the time I was isolated." Asked whether Alabama was the foundation for his music, he answered: *almost exclusively.*

This is the first piece of the production method. The hardcore-punk and noise-rock he absorbed in adolescence — Bad Brains, Fear, Black Flag — was, by his own framing, music that "matched the intensity of how I felt." It was not an aesthetic preference; it was an accommodation between an interior state and the available sonic vocabulary. The Brooklyn-South displacement created a young listener who had access to two musical genealogies — the New York hip-hop he was born into and the Southern punk-and-noise underground that found him second — and a temperament that wanted both at once. The rest of the catalog is what happens when those genealogies are forced into the same signal chain.

He enlisted in the Air Force at eighteen. The deployments ran through Iraq, Kuwait, Germany, North Africa, and finally Japan, where he was stationed long enough to produce and self-release the bulk of the Devon Hendryx discography: *Dreamcast Summer Songs* (2009), *Generation Y* (2011), *Joechillworld* (2011), *Lisa* (2011), *The Rockwood Escape Plan* (2012), the *❤* / *Heart Emoji* EP (2012), *The Ghost~Pop Tape* (2013). The Devon Hendryx era is the romantic / interior pole of the catalog and the period he has described as a "heavy bout with depression." It is also, importantly, where the technical muscle was built. He had been making beats since fifteen. The discharge — honorable, after he reportedly spoke against superior officers' abuse — closed the military chapter. Hearing Kanye West's *My Beautiful Dark Twisted Fantasy* while deployed, he has said, was "life-changing." This is the first appearance of the figure who will become the central knot in the post-2024 work.

The Brooklyn-to-South-to-military-to-Japan path produced two facts that are easy to underrate. First, by the time Hendricks moved to Baltimore in early 2015 to begin the JPEGMAFIA project in earnest, he had been a working producer for nearly half his life. Second, his musical education was assembled outside any of the institutional channels that produce most "experimental" rappers — no art school, no conservatory, no early industry mentorship, no New York or LA scene-of-origin. The reading, journalism, and self-direction had happened on Air Force time, in Japanese barracks, in chat rooms and YouTube comment sections. This is the production method's hidden formal logic. The work sounds the way it sounds because it was assembled by an autodidact with simultaneous access to Brooklyn rap, Southern punk, Japanese vaporwave residue, and the entire pre-2015 internet — and no constituency to please.

## III. The Bell Foundry and the Birth of Peggy

The Baltimore years were short — early 2015 to mid-2017 — but they did most of the artistic work. Hendricks moved into the orbit of the Bell Foundry, the DIY warehouse at 1539 N. Calvert Street. He performed there. He lived around it. He recorded there. The space was condemned by the city of Baltimore in December 2016, in the post–Ghost Ship-fire crackdown on DIY venues, an act of municipal triage that would reverberate through the *Veteran* opener "1539 N. Calvert" — a song that functions, as Le Guess Who?'s framing later observed, as a perfect eulogy to a hub that fostered a vibrant, hardscrabble DIY music and arts scene for years.

The two prefatory mixtapes — *Communist Slow Jams* (April 2015) and *Darkskin Manson* (May 2015) — were the pivot. They are the records on which the Devon Hendryx interiority becomes JPEGMAFIA exteriority — the same depression turned outward into political rage, the same lo-fi sampling instinct sharpened against police brutality, gentrification, the post-Freddie-Gray Baltimore. Three lyrical postures cohere here that will recur across every subsequent project: anti-police, anti-gentrification, anti-white-supremacist rage filtered through Black radical political vocabulary; a disorienting deployment of irony — "slow jams" as a label for screaming, "Manson" inverted as a signifier of Black force-against-whiteness; lo-fi sample collage as overt political weapon. The aesthetic and the politics are not separable, and never become so. The aesthetic *is* the politics, executed in audio.

*Black Ben Carson* (February 2016) is the first JPEGMAFIA studio album proper — released as a cassette on the UK micro-label Memorials of Distinction, named the best Baltimore album of 2016 by *Baltimore City Paper*, ignored by the national press at the time. The title is a sneer at Ben Carson's 2016 Republican primary campaign, and the album opens with the thesis it would build outward from: a Black artist needs to seize the visibility of the only Black candidate from the two major parties and weaponize it back. Side A is hip-hop in its rawest form — loud, angry, political, self-aware. Side B is the Hendryx interior making a permitted re-entry, calmer and cleaner. The two halves are the catalog in miniature. Every JPEGMAFIA solo album since 2016 has had a Side B — a sequence of tracks that admits the rage is a method, not the whole man. *Veteran*'s "Thug Tears" and "Macaulay Culkin." *AMHAC*'s entire back half. *LP!*'s "TRUST!" *I Lay Down My Life For You*'s Buzzy Lee features. The Side B is the load-bearing element that critics consistently single out as the album's emotional core. It is also the proof, against the frequent flat reading of him as a noise-rapper-and-troll, that the romantic mode is constitutive of the project.

By mid-2017 he had moved to Los Angeles, where *Veteran* was mixed and mastered. The album was released January 19, 2018, on Deathbomb Arc — the label most readers will recognize from its early association with Death Grips, clipping., and Julia Holter, which is to say the lineage was, at least administratively, already correct. Pitchfork's Paul A. Thompson wrote a 7.7 review that called the album "a remarkable exercise in sound and texture" and praised its capacity to take "white-hot riffs and the most distasteful parts of our national politics, chops them up, and somehow scatters them perfectly into place." The album would later be listed at #171 on Pitchfork's Best Albums of the 2010s. *Document Journal* called it the soundtrack of 2018. Across reviews the words that recur are *jarring*, *dissonant*, *cathartic*, *ahead*, and — repeatedly — *funny*. The humor is part of the technique. He has insisted on this from the first interview cycle: "I can rap about politics and make it a jiggy song." The political-as-jiggy axiom is the thing the rest of the catalog has been negotiating ever since.

## IV. Sample Warfare

The most consistent technical signature across every JPEGMAFIA release is what is best called *sample warfare* — the deliberate selection of source material whose semantic content stands in maximum tension with the lyric placed on top of it. This is not a metaphor; it is a method. The samples are not chosen for their groove or their familiarity, the way most hip-hop sampling has worked since the SP-1200 era. They are chosen for their *meaning* — and then the lyric is built to either contradict the meaning, ironize it, weaponize it, or surface a hidden meaning the original record did not know it contained. This is the load-bearing compositional decision underneath nearly every JPEGMAFIA song that lands.

The exemplar is "Real Nega," from *Veteran*. The bed of the song is built from Ol' Dirty Bastard's "Goin' Down" — ODB's most absurd, most playfully *unserious* vocal moment in the Wu-Tang catalog — bracketed by WWE's "You Think You Know Me" (the Edge entrance theme by Jim Johnston) and by Trap-A-Holics' DJ tags, with chanted "I'm a real n***a" vocals lifted from Chef & I's obscure novelty record "OOO (I'm a Reel Nigga)." On top of this, JPEGMAFIA performs a song about Black masculinity, alt-right confrontation, and the constructed nature of "realness." The technique is doing several things at once: an anthem of "real Black masculinity" stitched out of one of the silliest vocal moments in hip-hop history is a structural critique of the very category it is anthemizing; the wrestling theme is a parody of the entrance-music posture rappers borrow; the chant-along is a forced participation that registers as both ironic and earnest. The samples are the argument. The lyric is the verdict.

The pattern repeats across the catalog. "1488" — a song titled with the canonical white-supremacist double-numeric — uses a sample of Brad Paisley's "Make a Mistake," likely a sub-reference to the Brad Paisley / LL Cool J "Accidental Racist" embarrassment, with JPEGMAFIA inhabiting the symbol in order to detonate it. "Williamsburg" repurposes Travis Scott's "Butterfly Effect" — a song about touring opulence — into an anti-gentrification verse about selling art to yuppies. "BasicBitchTearGas" performs a near-direct cover of TLC's "No Scrubs" (1999) but inverts the gendered address: the female complaint about no-good men becomes, in JPEGMAFIA's hands, a verse about "basic bitches" — read by some critics as misogynist trolling, read by others, and by the artist himself, as ironic genre exercise built precisely so the irony cannot be evaded. "HAZARD DUTY PAY!" sets a track about industry "fakeness" against an Anita Baker & The Winans gospel sample, "Ain't No Need to Worry." "Burfict!" — named for former NFL linebacker Vontaze Burfict, one of the most violent players of his era — runs over a Richard Smallwood gospel interpolation. "Fentanyl Tester" is built on Kelis's "Milkshake." "SIN MIEDO" samples 2 Live Crew's "Hoochie Mama" and uses the bed for autobiographical writing about his father and his barber.

The technique is portable and consistent enough to function as authorial signature. It is also, importantly, *expensive*. The *LP!* sample-clearance crisis of 2021 — which forced the album into two distinct tracklists, *Online* and *Offline*, with different songs on each — is the direct consequence of the method. Hendricks has framed *Offline* as the "real" album: his attempt to "pull a Frank Ocean" on the label and keep his true artistic intentions in-house, free of clearance gates. Read structurally, the *Online/Offline* split is the cost of refusing to soften the technique. The samples are not negotiable. The art does not exist if they are removed.

This is also where the catalog's central technical limitation becomes visible. The sample-warfare method is a *cellular* compositional strategy — it operates at the bar level, the four-bar level, occasionally the eight-bar level, but rarely above. A JPEGMAFIA song is a sequence of incongruous cells, glued by his vocal and by the consistency of the mix. It is *cubist production*, in a recognizable sense — fragments collaged at the level of the bar, with no obligation to harmonic motion across the duration of the track. The technique produces extraordinary moments. It does not, on present evidence, produce *long forms*. There has not yet been a fifteen-minute JPEGMAFIA piece. There has not been an album-as-suite, in the manner of clipping.'s *Splendor & Misery*. The compositional vocabulary remains primitive in one specific sense — harmonic motion across a track is not, for him, a problem he is interested in solving. Whether that limitation is a position or a ceiling is the open question that the next record will, one way or another, answer.

## V. The Score Curve

The Pitchfork score arc across his five major solo records is monotonic and worth stating in a single line because the slope is doing more work than any individual review:

- *Veteran* (2018) — 7.7 (Paul A. Thompson)
- *All My Heroes Are Cornballs* (2019) — 7.6 (Stephen Kearse)
- *LP!* (2021) — 7.3 (Dylan Green)
- *Scaring the Hoes* (2023, with Danny Brown) — 7.1 (Pete Tosiello)
- *I Lay Down My Life For You* (2024) — 6.9 (Matthew Ritchie)

The Metacritic aggregate tracks the same softening: 85 → 82 → 86 (the Danny Brown collaboration as the high-water mark) → 78. The Fantano arc, reconstructed by fan consensus rather than verified from primary, is approximately the same shape: high-eights for *Veteran* and *AMHAC*, a softer mid-seven for *LP!*, an eight again for *Scaring the Hoes* (his highest JPEGMAFIA score and his direct framing of the album as the most chemistry-driven thing in the catalog), then his most ambivalent verdict on the *I Lay Down My Life For You* original — softened slightly on the Director's Cut release in February 2025.

What the slope is measuring is not, mostly, a decline in technical quality. The production on *Scaring the Hoes* is the best sustained beat-craft of his career. The mix on *I Lay Down My Life For You* is technically more accomplished than the mix on *Veteran*. The slope is measuring two other things, both of which are durable critic perceptions rather than mistakes. The first is *the diminishing returns of the persona*. Pitchfork's Matthew Ritchie wrote, of *ILDMLFY*, that "his extremely online subject matter takes the bloom off his writing" — this is the same observation that Stephen Kearse, six years and four albums earlier, was *praising*. Internet-poisoned rap, which felt like a discovery in 2018, is now legible as an authorial choice that is being made every record, and the freshness has been spent. The second is *the cost of the Director's Cut economy*. Once an artist starts releasing competing versions of the same album, every release reads as provisional. The original is no longer the canonical document; it is the first draft. The slope of the scores is, in part, the slope of provisionality.

The scores, importantly, have never been bad. A 6.9 from Pitchfork is the floor of the soft-affirmative range. The slope is interesting precisely because each individual review is positive. The cumulative effect — the fifth straight album that reviewers feel obligated to praise but cannot quite call essential — is what the next record is operating against. The reset, if there is one, has to address the slope's underlying causes. A more polished mix or a higher-profile feature will not bend the line. The line is bending because the persona has begun to overdetermine the writing, and the only way to bend it back is to write past the persona.

This is the project of "Free the Frail," continued. *Don't rely on the strength of my image.* The artistic test of the next record is whether he can write that line, twice, into the structure of the album.

## VI. Vultures: The Knot at the Center

The *Vultures 1* credits are the most consequential biographical event of the post-*Veteran* career, and the most revealing in what they expose about the catalog's animating contradiction. On February 10, 2024, Kanye West and Ty Dolla \$ign released *Vultures 1*. JPEGMAFIA wrote and produced four tracks: "Stars," "Fuk Sumn," "Beg Forgiveness," and "King." Those credits are not co-pros; they are not guest verses. They are full writing-and-production credits on what was, at the moment of release, the largest rap rollout of the year. They are also the most *composed* productions Hendricks has ever made — beds rather than collages, longer melodic phrases, harmonic motion that survives across the bar line. Working with West has visibly retuned his ear.

The participation was politically explosive. West's antisemitic statements of 2022–2023 had not been recanted; the *Vultures* rollout was contemporaneous with continued provocations, and would eventually escalate into the "HH-01" Yeezy shirt drop and the "Heil Hitler" track of May 2025. JPEGMAFIA had himself, in 2023, been on record telling an interviewer "Kanye's a Nazi now" — and he chose to take the credit anyway. He also issued, in the days around release, the most-quoted defense of his decision: "It's offensive to me that some people on here took a moment I had been waiting on my whole life and twisted into some weird ass oppression olympics." Read alongside the Cambridge Union remarks, which named West as his single biggest influence and cited *MBDTF* as the album he heard while deployed in the military and which he describes as "life-changing," the contradiction becomes structural. He cannot disown the influence; he refuses to apologize for the credit; he has tried, in the year since, to keep the music separate from the politics by stating publicly that "Ye's Twitter crashout is none of his business."

This is the knot. He has spent the entire JPEGMAFIA project insisting on the political content of his own art. He has named Trump, Tomi Lahren, Kellyanne Conway, Bill Maher, the Republican Party, Whole Foods liberalism, the alt-right's "incel" iconography. He has named Morrissey on a song title. He has, on *Veteran*, performed the curb-stomp of "Curb Stomp" against neo-Nazis as an explicit citation of the *American History X* scene. The political content is not subtext. And he chose to produce four tracks for the artist who, in the relevant year, was the most prominent antisemitic figure in mainstream music. He did this because the artist was Kanye West, and because the call-up to Kanye West was the call-up to "the majors." He has been honest about this — he has called the credits a "long-awaited call up to the majors" in his framing — and the honesty is the point. He is admitting on the record that the contradiction is one he chose to live inside.

*I Lay Down My Life For You* (August 2024) is the album-shaped consequence. The title is the most overtly Christian framing he has ever used. The cover and rollout for "PROTECT THE CROSS" foregrounds the American flag and frames the song as an exploration of patriotism from a former servicemember. The album has a Buzzy Lee feature on the title track and a Vince Staples feature on "New Black History" and a Denzel Curry feature on "JPEGULTRA!" who, by widespread critical reading, outraps him on his own song. The opener, "i scream this in the mirror before i interact with anyone," is a Death Grips reference (to "I Break Mirrors With My Face in the United States") and one of the most overtly autobiographical opening tracks in his catalog. NPR's review of the album was titled "JPEGMAFIA, rap's most tireless agitator, looks inward." Pitchfork called it "a frenetic album that tests the limits of his trolling," in which "JPEGMAFIA hints at the delicate balance of truth and fiction behind his confrontational mix of noise, rap, and punk."

What *I Lay Down My Life For You* could not yet do — and what the next record is structurally positioned to do — is metabolize the Kanye contradiction into the writing as the writing's actual subject. The 2024 album reaches for introspection but the introspection runs into the Kanye fallout sideways. The next record either takes the contradiction by its handle and writes the song that stops avoiding it, or it confirms that the contradiction is now the permanent state. The *AMHAC* thesis — *don't put your faith in artists you don't know* — was, by Hendricks' own framing to NPR, written about Kanye. The thesis still applies. The artist now has to apply it to himself.

## VII. The Director's Cut Decade

Beginning with *LP! Online vs. Offline* in 2021, Hendricks has released his solo records in increasingly elaborate provisional form. *LP!* was, by his own account, an album the label would not let him release as he wanted, and he split it into two tracklists rather than soften the samples. *Veteran: Director's Cut* arrived in June 2025, after he had recovered the master tracks — a victory-lap rerelease with bonus material, instrumentals, a lyric book, a PS2-style CD case, a cassette, and a 25-bullet-shaped-USB box set. *Scaring The Hoes: Director's Cut* arrived in October 2025. *I Lay Down My Life For You: Director's Cut* arrived in February 2025 with fourteen new tracks restructured as a two-disc set. *We Live in a Society* (May 2025) is a four-track Flume EP that reads, on RYM and AOTY, as a side-project bridge release.

Three Director's Cuts in three years is a pattern, not a series of one-offs. Read generously, it is the move of an artist with a deep-catalog instinct who refuses to let the standard album-rollout cycle close out his archives — a maximalist who treats every released project as a perpetually-unfinished site for additions, alternate versions, and reframings. Read less generously, it is the move of an artist who no longer trusts his own first drafts. The latter reading is the one Hendricks himself has begun to perform on social media. The June 3, 2025 X post announcing the *Veteran* master recovery — "Today, the master tracks for Veteran return to me. This is a privilege most artists do not get — the privilege of owning your work. This album changed my life forever. Without it, I would've never had the chance to showcase my experimental talents to the world. My albums are my children" — frames the Director's Cut economy as a sovereignty project. Owning your masters, recovering your back catalog, refusing to let the streaming version be the canonical version: these are real accomplishments and load-bearing for the political content of the work. *Infrastructure is the product*, in a phrase that does not appear in the JPEGMAFIA discourse but might as well.

But the production cost is real. Critics have begun to note that he is shipping the same tracks twice. Reviewers of the *I Lay Down My Life For You* Director's Cut are split between those who hear the bonus disc as a real improvement on the original (KTSW's positive read; the multiple references to "Bloodline Freestyle" as a standout) and those who hear it as bloat (the *Roosevelt Review*'s harsher verdict that the deluxe is "little more than half an hour of unorganized sounds, with much of the effort and artistry from the original album lost"). The Director's Cut model also begins to obscure what an artist's actual statement *is*. If two versions of *LP!* exist, neither is the album. If the original *I Lay Down My Life For You* is followed by a Director's Cut six months later, the original is, retrospectively, a beta version. The artist's first draft becomes, in the listener's experience, *not* the canonical text.

The next solo record either resolves this or extends it. A Director's Cut in 2027 of the May 2026 album — if the May 2026 album is real — would close the loop and confirm that the strategy is now permanent. A first draft that does not need a Director's Cut would be, in itself, a recovery of confidence — an admission that the artist trusts the version he is releasing. Whichever path he takes will be the most legible signal, in 2026, about what he intends to do with the rest of the decade.

## VIII. The K-Pop Test and the Pop-Producer Future

The two most important events of the post-*ILDMLFY* period are not albums. They are credits. *We Live in a Society* (May 2, 2025) is a four-track EP made entirely with Flume — the Australian producer best known for his Future Classic-era dance work, working independently of the label for the first time. The tracks include "We Live in a Society" itself, which opens as a self-aware parody of two production maximalists negotiating who gets to be loud; "Is It Real" with Ravyn Lenae, which sets JPEGMAFIA's stretched, auto-tuned vocals against Lenae's clean tone; "The Ocean is Fake," widely cited as the EP's standout; and "AI Girlfriend," whose video JPEGMAFIA co-directed with Logan Fields. Critical reception was mixed; the EP did not chart and was not given an album-level review at most major outlets.

The much more consequential credit arrived March 20, 2026. BTS's tenth studio album / sixth Korean-language LP, *Arirang*, includes a track called "Fya," co-written and co-produced by JPEGMAFIA alongside Diplo, Flume, and NITTI. The song is described as a Jersey-club leaning track, way out of left field for the group. The album also features Tame Impala (Kevin Parker), El Guincho, and others. *Arirang* is a major mainstream pop placement — it is JPEGMAFIA's first formal K-pop credit and the highest-volume listening any track he has ever produced will receive. K-pop A&R does not ship a track at the clipped, blown-out, red-lined master that "Real Nega" or "Baby I'm Bleeding" lives at. The mix has to compete on a Spotify-pop tier. The experience cannot be unlearned.

The 2024–2026 production trajectory — *Vultures 1* (Feb 2024) → *Vultures 2* credits → Flume EP (May 2025) → BTS *Arirang* (March 2026), with the Maxo Kream / Denzel Curry "Fake Jeezy" production credit slotted in early 2026 — is a man learning to produce *outside* the JPEGMAFIA solo project's signature sound. Each of those rooms required a different discipline. Kanye demanded composition and harmonic motion. Flume demanded hyperpop residue and post-genre legibility. BTS demanded a four-bar idea that could remain a four-bar idea for three minutes against a Jersey-club rhythm. Maxo Kream demanded Houston pocket. The next solo record will be made with all four of those disciplines audible in the producer's ear. He cannot return to being only an SP-404-and-FL-Studio bedroom producer; the bedroom is now too small.

The most important thing the K-pop test and the Flume EP have done is force a *cleaner mix*. The JPEGMAFIA signature has always lived at the edge of clipping — tape-saturated high-mid haze, kicks that cone the speaker, deliberate layer-stacking until the master red-lines. That is not a sound that competes on the same Spotify shelf as a BTS single. After *Arirang*, the home-studio crunch is now one of two mixes Hendricks has in his ears, and the next record will almost certainly land somewhere in between — cleaner low-end, tighter sub bass, less speaker-coning on the kicks, but with the tape-saturated vocal haze preserved as the recognizable JPEG signature. It will not be a *Yeezus*-clean mix; that is not him. It will be cleaner than *AMHAC*, where the mix was deliberately drowned. And the polish will be the most legible sonic difference between this record and everything that preceded it.

This is the test that worries the longest-running fans most. The cleaner mix is the sound of the artist becoming a working producer in genres other than his own. It is also, in industry-realist terms, the sound of him surviving past forty as a working professional. Pharrell figured this out. Timbaland figured it out. Kanye figured it out before he lost his mind. Hendricks is figuring it out at thirty-six, which is, for hip-hop production, right on schedule. The question is whether the JPEGMAFIA solo project — the one where the rage lives, the one where the political-as-jiggy axiom is enforced, the one where the samples are chosen for semantic incongruity — can survive the polish. There is no historical guarantee that it can. *Yeezus* is the closest example of an artist successfully importing avant-garde production discipline into a major-pop production sensibility, and *Yeezus* has not been replicated by anyone.

## IX. What "Experimental Rap" Means Now That It Is Here

The album is real. *EXPERIMENTAL RAP* is twenty-five tracks long, released April 30, 2026, on Hendricks' PEGGY imprint under exclusive license to AWAL. The title is doing the work that the album cover, the rollout, and the early metadata have only begun to clarify. It is a *Veteran*-scale gesture: seizing a descriptor that has been used against him for ten years and turning it into the album's frame. *Veteran* (2018) made the same move — taking the language of his service ("DD Form 214," "Hazard Duty Pay") and using it to describe the rap-game career, collapsing the two registers until the title meant both at once. *EXPERIMENTAL RAP* does the same to the genre tag itself. Hendricks has spent the entire JPEGMAFIA project either rejecting or being resigned to the descriptor. To title an album *EXPERIMENTAL RAP* in 2026 — at the moment when, by every objective measure (Vultures, Arirang, the Flume EP), he has become the most mainstream-adjacent artist to whom the descriptor still applies — is to claim authorship of the descriptor itself. It is to say: *I am the working definition. The category exists because I have made it exist. You do not get to use this word about me; I get to use it about myself.*

The rhetorical move is consistent with the catalog. *Veteran* was the discharge-document title turned into a thesis. *Black Ben Carson* was the candidate's name turned into a critique of the candidate. *All My Heroes Are Cornballs* was the disappointment thesis pre-loaded into the title — *don't put your faith in artists you don't know* was the album's argument, summarized for skim-reading. *I Lay Down My Life For You* was the Christian self-sacrifice phrase used as the title of his most secular-individualist album. Each title is a frame that the album then either honors or detonates. *EXPERIMENTAL RAP* is the most direct of these moves: a title that names the descriptor and dares the listener to find the album more or less experimental than the descriptor implies.

There is also the matter of the tracklist. As of release morning, twenty-four of the twenty-five tracks appear in public metadata as "Track 1," "Track 3," "Track 4," and so on — placeholders, ostensibly. The single track that surfaces with a name is track 2: *babygirl*, two minutes and twenty-seven seconds. If the placeholder names are a metadata-rollout artifact and the real titles arrive in the next twenty-four hours, that is its own kind of release-day tell — a man so committed to releasing on his date that he ships ahead of full DSP propagation. If, however, the placeholders are the titles — if the album is twenty-four numbered slots with one named song in the second position — then the gesture of *EXPERIMENTAL RAP* extends from the album cover into the structure of the document. An album titled *EXPERIMENTAL RAP* in which only one of twenty-five tracks has a name, and that name is *babygirl*, would be one of the more pointed conceptual gestures of his career. It would convert the album from a sequence of songs into a sequence of *positions*, with the romantic mode — the Helena Deland slot, the Buzzy Lee slot, the persistent Side B that has run through every JPEGMAFIA album since *Black Ben Carson* — finally given the only proper name on the record. *babygirl* would, on this reading, be the named center around which twenty-four numbered cells are arranged. That structure, if it holds, would be the most overt admission yet that the romantic mode is the actual subject of the work and the rage is the method.

The two readings cannot, on day-of-release, be adjudicated. They will be by the end of the week. What can be observed already is that either reading would be characteristic. Either is the kind of move Hendricks has spent ten years preparing for.

The descriptor has, by 2026, been hollowed out in mainstream usage. It now functions as a vibes-marketing category — *you wear a balaclava, you posted a Death Grips lyric in 2019* — rather than as a description of any specific compositional commitment. The genuine experimental-rap lineage is real and short — Anti-Pop Consortium, Dälek, cLOUDDEAD, Saul Williams, Death Grips, clipping., JPEGMAFIA — and most of what is currently sold as experimental rap is rap with weird drums. By the textural standard JPEGMAFIA is undeniably experimental: his production palette is among the most original of his generation. By the *compositional* standard he is borderline. Until *EXPERIMENTAL RAP* there had been no long-form piece. There had been no album-as-suite. He had worked almost exclusively in the three-minute song-with-hook format. He had been, on the available evidence, an extraordinary arranger of cells rather than a composer of structures.

Naming an album *EXPERIMENTAL RAP* is therefore a bet that the album itself will be required to settle. The available metadata gives us partial information. Twenty-five tracks at varying lengths, including several under sixty seconds and several past three minutes, suggests something other than the standard streaming-optimized format. The very short tracks — Track 1 at fifty-seven seconds, Track 18 at forty-four seconds, Track 22 at twenty-seven seconds — are either interludes or the cellular building blocks of something more ambitious in sequence. A 25-track album whose internal architecture treats some tracks as sub-minute fragments has more in common, structurally, with the cubist-collage logic of *Madvillainy* (2004) or with the long fragment-suites of clipping.'s *Splendor & Misery* (2016) than with the standard JPEGMAFIA solo album. Whether the fragments cohere as an album-as-suite, or as twenty-five independent provocations, will be the most useful single question to ask once the audio has been heard. The title commits to the suite. The metadata is consistent with the suite. The proof is in the listening.

There is a third reading, which is that the title is a private joke — that Hendricks is naming the album *EXPERIMENTAL RAP* the same way a Black artist would name an album *Real Hip-Hop* or *Authentic Rap*, with full awareness that the racial weight of the descriptor is what makes it grimly funny. White critics calling Black artists "experimental" for doing what hip-hop has been doing since *Paul's Boutique*, since *Bizarre Ride II the Pharcyde*, since DOOM, since Madvillain, since the Bomb Squad's twenty-source sample stacks for *It Takes a Nation of Millions to Hold Us Back* — this is a joke that JPEGMAFIA's catalog has been telling for a decade. The title is the joke in its most distilled form. The album is, in a recognizable Peggy gesture, both the joke and the proof that the joke is a real category.

## X. Three Records Inside The Twenty-Five Tracks

*EXPERIMENTAL RAP* has been public for hours, not days. No critic has yet written a full review. The audio has not yet been parsed for sample sources, lyrical content, or structural sequencing. What can be said in advance — and what this essay's framework can offer that a same-day review cannot — is the *listening frame*: three plausible versions of what the album turns out to be, against which the actual record can be evaluated as the listening windows accumulate over the next several weeks. The difference between them will be audible inside the first ninety seconds of Track 1. It is worth naming them.

The **coast-mode listening** is the one in which the K-pop polish flattens the rage, the lyrical introspection drifts into self-pity, the gospel samples become a tic rather than a technique, and the album resolves, in retrospect, as the platform from which the inevitable Director's Cut will be launched twelve months later. Sample selection runs to the safe end of the catalog: another Anita Baker-adjacent flip, another 2 Live Crew–adjacent flip, *babygirl* lands in the now-canonical Buzzy Lee / Helena Deland romantic slot, no Trump-2 protest cut because the protest energy of 2018 has not survived the *Vultures* contradiction. The 25-track length reads, on this listening, as bloat — the move that began with *I Lay Down My Life For You: Director's Cut*, made structural. Pitchfork: 6.5. The slope continues. Most disappointing scenario.

The **most-likely listening** is the strong-7 record. Three or four undeniable tracks. One rage-adjacent cut where the drum design absorbs the post-2hollis rage-scene palette (sidechained synth pads, gated reverb on the snares, Ken Carson / Yeat–derived hi-hat triplet placement) and then sabotages it with an SP-404 tape-stop or a gospel chop dropped in on the second drop. One gospel-flip in the "HAZARD DUTY PAY!" / "Burfict!" lineage. One romantic feature in the *babygirl* slot — track 2, the named song — that updates "Free the Frail" for 2026 and lands as the album's emotional core. One Trump-2 protest cut that comes from an exhausted rather than righteous register — the *I-told-you-so* of an artist who has been writing about this since 2016. A back half that drags. The sub-minute tracks (Track 1 at 0:57, Track 18 at 0:44, Track 22 at 0:27) read as interludes. A final track that gestures at resolving the Kanye-grief arc without quite getting there. Pitchfork: 7.0. The slope flattens. This is the realistic ceiling on present trajectory.

The **refocused listening** is the one where the contradictions become the writing — and where the 25-track structure is doing real architectural work rather than streaming-volume work. The sub-minute tracks are not interludes but the cellular building blocks of something more ambitious — a fragment-suite, in the lineage of *Madvillainy* or *Splendor & Misery*, where the bar-level cubism of the JPEGMAFIA production method finally finds the long-form structure it has always implied but never attempted. The Vultures-fallout / Kanye-grief metabolizes into the lyric in a way that makes *I Lay Down My Life For You*'s introspection look like a sketch of what *this* album is doing on purpose. *babygirl* — the only named song — is the named center around which the numbered fragments are arranged, and its position at track 2 is the structural admission that the romantic mode has been the work's actual subject all along. The drum programming integrates rage-scene weight without losing the JPEGMAFIA identity. The cleaner BTS-trained mix gives the maximalist sample stacks more headroom, and they hit harder in the cleaner space rather than dissolving. The album does not need a Director's Cut, and the audience knows it does not, and Hendricks does not release one. Pitchfork: 8.0. The slope reverses. This is the *Veteran*-scale event.

The most likely outcome is the second. The reason it is not the third is that the third requires a structural commitment — to long-form composition, to writing past the persona, to refusing the Director's Cut in twelve months — that the catalog has not yet demonstrated the capacity for, and that *EXPERIMENTAL RAP* will only retroactively prove possible if the audio holds up to the framework the title and the tracklist seem to set up. The reason the second is most likely is that the second is the path of least resistance for an artist who has been on this path for five albums. The reason the first cannot be ruled out is that coast-mode is the equilibrium state of any artist who has had a five-album decline corrected, twice, by a collaboration with a more chemistry-driven partner (Kenny Beats, Danny Brown), and *EXPERIMENTAL RAP* is — uniquely among the post-*Veteran* solo records — entirely his own document, with no chemistry-correcting collaborator on the writing.

The *Scaring the Hoes Vol. II* question, which seemed live two weeks ago, becomes secondary now. Vol. II remains "in the works," per Brown's April 2025 announcement; no release date has been disclosed. With *EXPERIMENTAL RAP* shipping today, Vol. II almost certainly arrives later in 2026 or in 2027 — which means *EXPERIMENTAL RAP* stands alone as the solo statement, evaluated on its own terms, without the chemistry-album bounce that consistently elevated the catalog at *Scaring the Hoes* in 2023. Whichever of the three readings turns out to be correct, the album lives or dies in the next several weeks of listening on its own merit. There is no Danny Brown verse to carry it. There is no Kanye production credit to translate it. There is no Director's Cut yet to revise it. There are twenty-five tracks, one named song, and the title.

## XI. The Loop Is The Subject

The most interesting line in the Crack Magazine cover story from 2019 is one Hendricks himself volunteered, when asked about mortality. *I'm just gonna wither away. I just think about it every now and then. I'm gonna wither away and be nothing.* Then, in the same paragraph: *If I've made a body of work and done enough in my life to where some kid a hundred years from now can say "I remember JPEGMAFIA," that'd be enough for me.* The two sentences are the most distilled version of the contradiction the catalog has been working through since 2016. The first is the Devon Hendryx interior — depression, isolation, the *Ghost~Pop Tape* mortality reflex. The second is the JPEGMAFIA exterior — the persona constructed precisely so that, in a hundred years, a kid will remember the persona. *Don't rely on the strength of my image* says one thing. *I'm gonna wither away and be nothing* says the other. *I want some kid to remember JPEGMAFIA in a hundred years* says the third. The work has been the negotiation between them.

The actual avant-garde gesture in JPEGMAFIA's career may not be musical. It may be biographical. The career itself is structured as a sample flip. The samples are the lived material — a Brooklyn childhood, a Southern adolescence, an Air Force tour, a Baltimore DIY scene, an LA major-label move, a Kanye production credit, a BTS placement — and the lyric on top is the persona that has been chopped, layered, and reframed across every album since *Black Ben Carson*. The Director's Cuts are not an artistic failure on this reading; they are an extension of the method. Every album is now a sample to be flipped again. The career is the loop, and the loop is the subject. *EXPERIMENTAL RAP* is the loop being named.

Whether the album resolves or extends the contradictions is the only question that matters. The contradictions are not a bug. They are the engine. Hendricks has spent ten years insisting that politics and danceability are not in opposition; that vulnerability and rage can coexist on the same record; that the rapper can be both the troll and the writer; that a Black artist can produce for Kanye West in the year of *Vultures 1* and still hold political commitments that condemn the participation. Those insistences are not slogans. They are the structural architecture of the catalog. The next record either confesses that some of those insistences have begun to hollow out — that the political-as-jiggy axiom, after Trump 2, runs into limits the 2018 axiom did not anticipate; that the Vultures contradiction cannot be metabolized without admission; that the persona has begun to overdetermine the writing — or it doubles down and treats the contradictions as permanent.

The quieter possibility, which neither of the first two readings captures, is that Hendricks already knows all of this and has known it for at least three albums. *AMHAC* was, on his own framing, a "disappointment" record made in opposition to expectation. The artist who titled "Free the Frail" the way he did in 2019 is not surprised by Pitchfork's "the bloom off his writing" verdict in 2024. The career has been transparent about its own arc. The next record, on this reading, is not a crisis to be resolved but a chapter that is being written from inside the same self-awareness that wrote every prior chapter. There will be a Side B. There will be a Buzzy Lee feature. There will be a sample chosen for semantic incongruity. There will be a song that lands harder than its setup and a song that lands softer. There will, almost certainly, be a Director's Cut twelve months later. The pattern does not break.

What the next record can do — and this is the project that "Free the Frail" began and that *I Lay Down My Life For You* did not finish — is bring the loop into the open and write *about* the loop. Make the persona visible to itself. Stop hedging the first draft. The artist who recovered his *Veteran* masters and wrote, in the most unguarded social-media post of his career, *my albums are my children*, is an artist who has the language for the move. Whether he commits to it is the only fact in 2026 that is not yet written.

The album arrived April 30, 2026 — three weeks earlier than the rumored date, on his own imprint, twenty-five tracks long, with a single named song called *babygirl* and the rest of the document still resolving its names into public view at the moment of writing. The next several weeks of listening will tell us which of the three readings holds. So will, in the longer run, the question of whether a Director's Cut follows. The last sample is the artist's own catalog. The last lyric on top of it is the one he has been writing for a decade and has — finally — given a name to.

*Don't rely on the strength of my image.* The strongest reading of the line, in 2026, is that the artist meant it as a request, and that *EXPERIMENTAL RAP* is the answer he is now willing to make in public — twenty-five tracks of audio that the audience will spend the rest of the year reading against the persona that has been the loop's lyric since 2016, and against the descriptor that is now, finally, his.

Three speculative analyses then took both dossiers as input — a critical/production reading, a compositional-formal reading, and an industry-and-rapping reading — to triangulate the catalog's trajectory and the most plausible shape of the May 2026 record. Where their readings converged, the essay states the convergence as part of the argument; where they diverged, the essay either preserves the disagreement as an open question or follows the reading that has the most direct support in the primary record. The three readings did not produce a debate to be summarized; they produced a sharper picture of the artist's actual situation in 2026, and the essay is built around that picture.
