# Counting the Uncountable: A Listener's Guide to Time in Jazz and Hip-Hop

*Part 1 of a series on rhythm in Black American music.*

---

## The seed

I had been sitting with *Live-Evil* (Miles Davis, 1971) again — the electric-era Miles record that always seemed to me, when I was younger, like the meters were doing something I couldn't follow. Friends would say it sounded like "odd time," and I would nod, and go back to it, and the count would slip away every time.

It took years to understand that the count was slipping away because there was no odd count to find.

The meters on *Live-Evil* are mostly loose and funk-driven. The studio tracks — "Sivad," "Little Church," "Gemini/Double Image" — sit in 4/4, but played so elastically that the bar lines blur. Michael Henderson's bass vamps hold the floor. Jack DeJohnette plays broken funk across them. Airto Moreira's percussion floats on top, dragging in Brazilian cross-rhythms. You'd count 4/4, but the accents float anywhere they want.

"What I Say" is the cleanest case. A relentless 4/4 funk vamp from Henderson — and DeJohnette playing across it constantly, so the whole thing *feels* polyrhythmic even though it isn't. You lock to the bass, not the drums. "Inamorata" stretches similarly: long modal vamps where the ensemble plays *with* the pulse rather than against a fixed odd meter. If you're hearing something that feels uncountable on this record, it's almost never a 7 or an 11. It's 4/4 with layered cross-rhythm, African and Latin percussion overlays, and Miles, Hermeto Pascoal, and Keith Jarrett soloing in phrases that ignore the bar.

The Brubeck "count the 5" approach doesn't apply here. *Live-Evil* is closer to James Brown than to "Take Five." Find the one in the bass, and let the rest wash over you.

That's the diagnostic problem this essay is about. There are at least three different things going on when music "sounds weird," and most listeners — and a lot of musicians who should know better — collapse them into one. I want to take them apart.

---

## The three diagnoses

Before any history, the punchline. When a piece of music sounds like the count is slipping, it is usually one of three things:

**(a) Actual odd meter.** The bar is genuinely 5 or 7 or 11 beats long. The cycle keeps coming back on that count. You can tap it once you find it.

**(b) 4/4 with cross-rhythm or implied 3-over-4.** The bar is 4 beats, but groupings of 3 or 5 or 6 are layered on top so that the surface accents don't line up with the downbeat. Most jazz that "sounds complicated" lives here.

**(c) 4/4 with micro-timing displacement.** The bar is 4 beats, the groupings are normal, but individual hits sit slightly behind or ahead of where a metronome would put them. The pulse is stable; the *placement* is the variable.

These three categories have radically different histories, radically different techniques, and produce radically different feels. Calling all of them "polyrhythmic" or "syncopated" or "off" is the listener equivalent of calling every jazz chord "a 7th." It collapses the thing you wanted to talk about.

The rest of this essay is a slow walk through how jazz and hip-hop arrived at each of these zones, and a small toolkit for telling them apart by ear.

## Part one: jazz, and the slow opening of the bar

### Bebop's 4/4 floor

Modern jazz, for most of its first fifty years, lives in 4/4. Bebop is 4/4. Hard bop is 4/4. Cool jazz is 4/4. The complexity of the music sits almost entirely in harmony, melody, and the *micro-rhythmic* relationship between the swing eighth and the cymbal pulse — not in the meter itself.

The first time the bar starts to open up in any sustained way is the jazz waltz.

### The jazz waltz: 3/4 as a normal place to live

In 1956, Sonny Rollins recorded "Valse Hot" on *Sonny Rollins Plus 4* — among the earliest modern jazz waltzes, played as if 3/4 were a place a small group could improvise inside without it being a stunt. A year later, Max Roach released *Jazz in 3/4 Time* (EmArcy, 1957), the first full LP devoted to the meter. This is two years *before* Brubeck's *Time Out*, which is the record everyone associates with jazz playing in something other than 4.

What's striking about the waltz period is how unstrained it is. Bill Evans' "Waltz for Debby" — composed in 1956 for his niece, canonically captured live in 1961 — plays the head in 3/4, and then, around 1:04, *quietly shifts to 4/4 for the solos*, returning to 3/4 for the out-head. The meter switch is not the point of the piece. It's a structural detail, the way a key change might be. The 3/4 framing is a nursery-rhyme reference to a child, not a difficulty to overcome.

John Coltrane's "My Favorite Things" (1960) does something similar: head and modal vamp solos in 3/4 (or 6/4, depending on who's notating), but the innovation everyone remembers is the *harmonic stasis* — a soprano sax orbiting a single chord — not the meter. The waltz is the vehicle. The thing being delivered is something else.

So 3/4 in jazz is, by 1960, a normal place to be.

### Brubeck, Ellis, and the import of asymmetric meter

The story of *odd* time in jazz — 5/4, 7/4, 9/8, 11/8 — runs through two specific channels, and it's worth keeping them separate.

The first channel is the Brubeck channel: a deliberate, ethnomusicological import of Eastern European and Turkish meters. *Time Out* (1959) is the canonical document. Two pieces matter here.

"Take Five" (composed by Paul Desmond at Brubeck's request as a vehicle for drummer Joe Morello) is in 5/4 throughout — head, sax solo over an Eb minor / Bb minor 7 vamp, drum solo, out-head. The 5 is grouped 3+2, with accents on beats 1 and 4. The piece never resolves to 4/4. It commits.

"Blue Rondo à la Turk," on the same record, is more ambitious. The head alternates three bars of 9/8 grouped 2+2+2+3 (this asymmetric grouping is what musicologists call **aksak**, the Turkish word for "limping" — a meter where the beats are unequal in length) with one bar of 9/8 grouped 3+3+3. Brubeck attributed the rhythm explicitly to hearing Turkish street musicians during his 1958 State Department tour through Istanbul.

But here's the detail worth lingering on: when Brubeck's sextet hits the *blowing* section of "Blue Rondo," they drop the 9/8 entirely and play in straight 4/4. The out-head returns to 9/8. In 1959, the soloists weren't yet fluent enough in 9/8 to improvise inside it, so they code-switched. That code-switch is itself essay-worthy. It tells you exactly where the frontier was.

Don Ellis pushed the frontier further. By the mid-1960s — having begun graduate ethnomusicology study at UCLA in 1964 with the Indian musician Harihar Rao — Ellis was building entire books around meters that no one else was attempting. "33 222 1 222," recorded *Live at Monterey!* (1966), is in 19/4, and the title literally spells out the subdivision (3+3+2+2+2+1+2+2+2). "Bulgarian Bulge" alternates 33/16 and 36/16, with internal subdivisions like 15/8 (2+2+2+2+3+2+2) plus 9/8 plus 9/8.

Ellis is the cleanest case in 1960s jazz of cross-cultural rhythmic transfer: a working improviser doing the academic homework, and bringing back grids that Western popular music had no precedent for.

This is the *import* lineage. It's a relatively small slice of jazz, and its provenance is largely Balkan, Turkish, and South Indian.

### The other channel: African polyrhythm via Cuba

The second channel is older, larger, and frequently misdescribed.

Mongo Santamaría composed "Afro Blue" in 1959; it was first recorded that April by the Cal Tjader Sextet. It is, in the words of the standard description, "the first jazz standard built on a typical African 3:2 cross-rhythm" — what Western theory calls a **hemiola** (Greek for *one and a half*; the simplest polyrhythm; Bach used it at sarabande cadences).

Here is the technical heart of "Afro Blue": the bass plays six cross-beats per measure of 12/8 — a 6:4 polyrhythm, which decomposes into two cells of 3:2. The piece is *not in odd meter*. It's in 12/8, which is a perfectly symmetric compound meter. What makes it sound the way it does is the systematic layering of one grouping (sixes) against another (fours), so the downbeats align but the internal grids do not.

This is what jazz players grow up calling "Latin," and what ethnomusicologists more accurately call **cross-rhythm**: the African organizing principle in which the layered groupings *are* the structure, not deviations from a default meter. In a West African ensemble, the bell pattern doesn't decorate a 4/4 — it generates the time-feel. The 4/4 is something a Western listener overlays after the fact.

Coltrane and Elvin Jones flipped "Afro Blue" in 1963, recasting it as a 3/4 swing — re-orienting the same cross-rhythm in a different frame. The cross-rhythm is robust to the framing, which is part of why it works.

This is the lineage that runs through most of what *Live-Evil* is doing. It's not the Brubeck import. It's the Cuban-mediated African inheritance — with Brazilian percussion (Airto) and electric funk (Henderson) added on top.

### The classic-quintet move: implied meter and metric modulation

Inside the bebop-and-after 4/4, the great rhythm-section innovators of the 1960s found another way to open the bar: they played *as if* the meter had changed, while leaving it formally intact.

Elvin Jones with Coltrane is the textbook case. Jones implies 3 over the quartet's 4 so consistently that listeners often hear the band as "in 3" when the bass is unambiguously walking in 4. This is **implied meter** — a soloist or drummer phrasing as if in a different meter while the rhythm section holds the actual one. There is no genuine simultaneous grid here, only the implication.

Tony Williams, in Miles' Second Quintet, took this further by importing **metric modulation** — the technique, named by critic Richard Franko Goldman about Elliott Carter's Cello Sonata (1948), of pivoting from one tempo to another via a shared subdivision: a triplet eighth in the old tempo becomes a straight eighth in the new one. Carter himself preferred "tempo modulation," but the term that stuck was Goldman's. Williams brought it into jazz on *Miles Smiles*. Davis later said Williams was "the center the group's sound revolved around," and it's hard to overstate this — Williams was nineteen years old.

The clearest example is Wayne Shorter's "Footprints" (*Miles Smiles*, 1966). The piece is written in 6/4, but on the recorded performance, around 2:20 into Miles' solo, Williams transforms the 6/8 feel into 4/4 — same pulse, different grouping. The piece's *identity* is the moving target between 3-grouping and 2-grouping. There is no fixed meter to lock to. The meter is a verb.

### Fusion: meter as deliberate composition

By the early 1970s, two things are happening at once. The Mahavishnu lineage — McLaughlin coming out of Miles' electric band, then studying with Ravi Shankar and L. Shankar — is composing genuine odd meter as a structural choice. *Birds of Fire* (1973) opens on 18/8 (two bars of 9/8) and transitions to a 13/8 vamp built as a descending stroke pattern of 6-5-4-3-2. *The Dance of Maya* (Inner Mounting Flame, 1971) cycles through what's often transcribed as 20/8.

This is what most listeners *think* fusion-era electric jazz sounds like. And for Mahavishnu, it's accurate.

But Miles, on the same continent, is doing something completely different. *Live-Evil* lives almost entirely in 4/4. Weather Report's "Birdland" (1977) is 4/4. Most of Weather Report's catalog is 4/4. The disorientation in this music is category (b) — cross-rhythm and implied meter on top of a stable bar — not category (a).

The two streams flow in parallel for the rest of the decade and never quite merge.

### The modern moment: explicit and implicit, side by side

In contemporary jazz, both streams are still alive, and the interesting artists are often the ones who can articulate which is which.

Vijay Iyer's "Inertia" sits in 7/4, with quarter-note chords in the left hand. Iyer has described elsewhere a 9-beat cycle in his work that "sounds like 4 plus 5" but plays so smoothly it doesn't register as odd. The lineage Iyer points to is Steve Coleman's M-Base, which Iyer has called "as important as Coltrane" — meaning, in part, that M-Base was the place where rigorous odd-meter improvising became a normal vocabulary for a generation of Black American improvisers.

Tigran Hamasyan goes further. His meters, derived from Armenian folk dance, are fully cataloged in a peer-reviewed analysis (Schumann, *Music Theory Online* 27.2, 2021): "Drip" (4/4 with implied 5/4 in an 8:5 ratio); "Song for Melan and Rafik" (seven bars of 5/8 plus one of 7/8); "A Crane Came From Van" (6/16 + 6/16 + 7/16); "Entertain Me" (alternating 20/16 and 15/16); "Vardavar" (5/16+5/16+3/16+5/16+5/16+4/16+5/16); "The Grid" (5/16+5/16+7/16+5/16+5/16+5/16). This is Don Ellis territory, fifty years on, with the ethnomusicological scaffolding fully internalized.

Rudresh Mahanthappa's *Kinsmen* (2008), with the late Carnatic saxophonist Kadri Gopalnath, takes South Indian *tala* cycles as compositional starting points — the konnakol/tihai vocabulary (vocalized rhythmic syllables; *tihai* = a phrase repeated three times engineered to land back on beat 1, the *sam*) translated into a jazz quintet's blowing language.

And then there's Makaya McCraven, whose *In These Times* (2022) is one of the rare modern records where the artist himself publicly distinguishes the categories. McCraven has said the album is built around 7/8, 5/8, and 11/8 grids, with a few 4/4 tracks "for good measure." The phrasing itself is the diagnostic. He knows which category each track is in.

On the other side, the artists who live deliberately in category (b) and (c) — Robert Glasper, Kamasi Washington, Mark Guiliana, Brad Mehldau (whose Radiohead covers preserve the 10/4 of "Everything In Its Right Place" and the 7/8 sections of "Paranoid Android," but whose original work is overwhelmingly 4/4) — are doing something different. The complexity has migrated from the bar line to the *placement* of the hits inside the bar.

Which is, finally, the thing hip-hop has been doing all along.

## Part two: hip-hop, where the meter is almost never the point

If you want to understand hip-hop's relationship to time, the worst thing you can do is look at the time signature. It's almost always 4/4. The interesting rhythmic information lives one or two layers below the bar.

### Roger Linn and the swing percentage

The mechanical history matters here. Roger Linn designed the LinnDrum (1982), then the Akai MPC60 (1988), then the MPC3000 (1994). On these machines, he introduced a parameter that has shaped almost every hip-hop record since: **swing**, expressed as a percentage of an eighth-note duration given to the first sixteenth versus the second.

50% swing is no swing — perfectly straight sixteenths. 66% is a perfect triplet shuffle, the feel of jazz swing or a Texas blues. The territory Linn opened up is the in-between: 54% to 62%, the zone Linn himself described as "loosening up the feel of straight sixteenths without sounding like swing."

That in-between zone is most of 90s boom-bap.

### Boom bap: tempo around 90, swing around 56%

DJ Premier, Pete Rock, Large Professor — the canonical East Coast producers — are working in straight 4/4 at 85 to 95 BPM, with swung sixteenths via the SP-1200 or the MPC60. The funk, the thing that makes the records feel like they're walking instead of marching, lives in details like a hi-hat displaced by a 32nd note before beat 3. RZA, by reputation, didn't even use a click track on much of his early work; he detuned and pitched down soul samples that float against the drums in ways that no quantize grid would tolerate.

None of this shows up on a notated time signature. All of it is what makes the records sound like the records.

### The Dilla paradigm shift

The cleanest articulation of what changes with J Dilla comes from Dan Charnas's *Dilla Time* (2022), which proposes that there are now three categories of time in popular music: straight time, swing time, and **Dilla time** — *multiple feels happening at once, some elements straight, some swung, some on the grid, some intentionally ahead or behind.*

The standard misreading of Dilla — that he "just turned off quantize" — is exactly wrong. Charnas pushes back hard on this, and the technical detail matters: Dilla used **selective quantization** and the MPC's nudge function to displace specific elements by specific amounts. And crucially, *he looped the same micro-timing deviations throughout a song.* That repetition is what makes it intentional rather than sloppy. A drunk drummer is random. Dilla is systematic.

The architectural move that defines the style: **the snare stays locked to the grid (the body's anchor), the kick and hats float.** On "Workinonit" (*Donuts*, 2006), academic micro-timing analyses describe the kick hitting a hair late and the snare creeping slightly early, with anticipations on the order of a 32nd-note duration. On "Stakes Is High" (De La Soul, 1996) — one of the early mainstream documents of the style, built on chops from Ahmad Jamal's "Swahililand" — the same logic is already in place.

This is category (c) in the diagnostic. The bar is 4/4. The groupings are normal eighths and sixteenths. The disorientation comes from where the hits *sit*.

### Madlib and equipment-induced wobble

Madlib's contribution is adjacent and worth distinguishing. *Madvillainy* (2004) was made on a Boss SP-303 plus a portable turntable plus a tape deck, in a Brazilian hotel room. The SP-303's pitch instability — combined with Madlib's preference for sampling sources with the effect already printed — gave the loops an irreproducible waver.

There's also a sample-chopping dimension that gets misread frequently. On "Raid," Madlib sampled a Brazilian jazz track in 3/4, pitched it down, and chopped it into a 4/4 frame. The original meter dissolves. What survives is the *swing-feel* of the source — the phrasing accents — recast as syncopation inside a 4/4 grid. This is a category-(b) move (forcing one meter inside another), not a category-(a) move (working in odd meter), and it's frequently mistaken for the producer being clever about meter when actually the producer is being clever about loops.

The general principle: when you chop a 6/8 soul-jazz waltz onto 16th-note triggers in a 4/4 grid, the bar line of the original is destroyed but the *accents* are preserved. The result sounds rhythmically rich because it carries the ghost of a different meter without ever stating one.

### Trap: a two-clock feeling inside one bar

Trap, codified by Lex Luger on Waka Flocka's *Flockaveli* (2010) and pushed forward by 808 Mafia, Southside, and Metro Boomin, is technically in 4/4 at around 140 BPM. But the kick and snare backbeat pulls toward half-time (~70 BPM), while the hats run double-, triple-, or quadruple-time — sixteenths, triplets, sometimes 32nds, with the 32nd and 64th rolls before the snare or before bar transitions doing the dramatic work.

The "drag" of trap is this two-clock feeling: a slow body-pulse and a fast hand-pulse inside the same bar. It is, in a precise sense, an *internal polyrhythm* — different groupings in the same span of time — but one that doesn't show up on a notated time signature. If you transcribe a trap beat, you'll write 4/4. The polyrhythmic character is in the *layering* of subdivisions, not in the meter.

### The rare meter exception: "Hey Ya!"

Hip-hop almost never plays in odd meter, which is part of what makes Outkast's "Hey Ya!" (2003) such a famous outlier. The pattern is 4/4 + 4/4 + 4/4 + 2/4 + 4/4 + 4/4 — twenty-two beats over what would otherwise be six bars. You can read it as a single bar of 11/4 split across two phrases; most analysts call it mixed meter with one truncating bar of 2/4.

The 2/4 bar is what produces the weird forward push of the song. It feels like the chorus is shoving you into the next phrase a beat early — because, structurally, it is. The reason "Hey Ya!" gets cited so often as the meter exception in hip-hop is that hip-hop almost never does this. The genre's rhythmic invention happens elsewhere.

### Neo-soul: Dilla translated to live instruments

The Soulquarians sessions at Electric Lady that produced D'Angelo's *Voodoo* (2000) are the live-instrument translation of what Dilla was doing at the MPC. Questlove on drums, Pino Palladino on bass, Dilla as a hands-on collaborator.

Questlove on Dilla: "He's the zenith of hip-hop to us. Jay Dee helped to bring out the album's dirty sound and encouraged the false starts and the nonquantized sound of the record."

Pino Palladino: "When I first heard the backing tracks for *Voodoo*, it struck me as the kind of thing J Dilla would do, how he would deconstruct and reconstruct rhythms and just kinda deliberately mess things up."

The technical move is **backphrasing** — what Raphael Saadiq calls "playing with the grease." Questlove plays straight, heavy, locked drums. Pino constantly shifts his bass position relative to those drums. The bass is a moving target against a fixed backbeat. This is the inverse of an MPC pattern, where the snare anchors and the kick floats — but conceptually it's the same move. One element is the anchor; another element is allowed to drift around it; the relationship between them is what produces the "feel."

This is why *Voodoo* and *Donuts* feel like cousins. They're not in odd meter. They're not even doing layered cross-rhythm in the African sense. They're doing something more recent and, in some ways, more radical: holding a stable 4/4 grid and letting individual hits negotiate their own placement inside it.

### Glasper as the bridge

When Robert Glasper said, "I learned how to stay in a pocket, and understand how to play piano like a sample, by playing with Questlove," he was identifying the connecting tissue between the two halves of this essay. Glasper's *Black Radio* (2012), which won the Grammy for Best R&B Album, is the canonical document of jazz musicians internalizing hip-hop's micro-timing vocabulary. The album is overwhelmingly 4/4. What makes it sound the way it does is Chris Dave's drumming — late hi-hats, dropped backbeats, Dilla-style quantization — applied inside a jazz-piano context.

This is the inverse of Mahavishnu. With McLaughlin, the meter is the most complicated thing on the page. With Glasper, the meter is the simplest thing on the page; the complexity has migrated entirely into placement.

The same goes for Kamasi Washington's *The Epic* (2015), most of which is in 4/4 or 3/4 with two drummers (Tony Austin and Ronald Bruner Jr.) creating "angular polyrhythms" on top of a stable bar. Mark Guiliana's work with Mehliana lives in 4/4 with subdivision as the variable.

The story of contemporary jazz, in its hip-hop-influenced wing, is the story of the bar line getting *less* interesting, and the placement of hits inside the bar getting *more* interesting. Which is, again, what hip-hop was doing all along.

## Part three: the listener's toolkit

Here is the diagnostic, restated as something you can actually do.

### Tap a slow steady pulse and listen to where it lands.

If the cycle keeps coming back to the same count and that count is 5 or 7 or 9 or 11, you are in **category (a) — actual odd meter**. The bar genuinely has an unusual number of beats in it. *Take Five* (5/4), *Birds of Fire* (18/8 → 13/8), most of Tigran Hamasyan, much of Don Ellis, much of Makaya McCraven's *In These Times*. The cycle is the diagnostic. It comes back where it comes back.

If the cycle comes back on 4 but the *surface* of the music is grouped in 3s or 5s or 6s — if the bass anchor confirms the 4 while the soloists or percussion are spinning in different groupings on top — you are in **category (b) — 4/4 with cross-rhythm or implied meter**. *Afro Blue* (12/8 with 6:4 cross-rhythm), *Footprints* (the 6/4 ↔ 4/4 ambiguity), *most of Live-Evil*, *most of late Coltrane*, James Brown, the Cuban-Brazilian inheritance.

If the cycle comes back on 4, the groupings are normal, but individual hits feel slightly behind or ahead of where a metronome would put them — if the *pulse* feels stable but the *placement* doesn't — you are in **category (c) — 4/4 with micro-timing displacement**. *Donuts*, *Voodoo*, *Black Radio*, boom bap with Linn-style swing, trap with its two-clock feeling, the entire Soulquarians arc.

### A test for category (c) specifically.

Take a beat that sounds alive to you and program it quantized in a DAW — every hit on the grid, no swing, no nudge. If the quantized version sounds dead and the original sounds alive, the difference between them is category (c). The information is in the placement, and your ear is hearing it even when you can't name it.

### A listening exercise

Put on "What I Say" from *Live-Evil*. Find Henderson's bass. Tap quarter notes with it. Now listen to DeJohnette. He is not playing those quarter notes. He is grouping in something else — implying triplets, throwing accents into spaces where the bar doesn't expect them. The relationship between your tapping foot and DeJohnette's hands is the cross-rhythm.

Then put on "Take Five." Tap until you find the 5. Notice how the cycle resets cleanly every five beats. The accents are *on* the count (1 and 4 of the 3+2 grouping). Your foot agrees with the music about where the bar starts.

Then put on "Workinonit" from *Donuts*. Tap quarter notes. The pulse is right there. But listen to the kick — it's a hair late. Listen to the snare — it's a hair early. Your tapping foot is still right; the music is what's negotiating with the foot.

Three different relationships between you and the time. Three different categories of "weird."

### Why this distinction matters

Mostly because it lets you hear what's actually happening. There is a particular kind of lazy listening that calls all of this "polyrhythmic" or "off" or "complicated," and that listening flattens out the difference between Don Ellis transcribing Bulgarian folk dance and J Dilla nudging a kick drum. They are not the same act. They came from different places, they require different skills, and they produce different feelings.

But there's also a making-music reason. If you're a player or a producer trying to learn from any of this, the wrong diagnosis sends you to the wrong woodshed. If you hear *Live-Evil* as odd meter and try to learn 11/8, you will learn something useful but you will not be closer to *Live-Evil*. If you hear *Donuts* as a sample-meter trick and try to chop loops cleverly, you will not be closer to Dilla. The technique follows the diagnosis. Misdiagnose and you learn the wrong instrument.

## A note on the polymeter debate

Worth flagging in passing, because it comes up in any honest discussion of category (b).

A working set of definitions:

- **Polyrhythm**: two rhythmic groupings sounding simultaneously inside the same span of time, divided in incommensurate ways. *Downbeats align, the grid lines don't.* (3:2, 4:3, 6:4)
- **Polymeter**: two simultaneous meters with different bar lengths but a shared subdivision. *Grid lines align, downbeats don't.* (3/4 against 4/4 with a constant eighth note)
- **Cross-rhythm**: the African ethnomusicological term — systematic, ostinato layering against an underlying pulse. In African ensemble practice, the cross-rhythm *is* the basic structure, not a deviation from a default meter.

Some pedagogical sources defend the polyrhythm/polymeter distinction as load-bearing. Others — including the Wikipedia entry, citing African music scholars — argue that much of what's labeled "polymeter" is really cross-rhythm wearing two time signatures, because there's almost always one underlying pulse that the listener locks to. (Try to listen to a "polymetric" 3/4-against-4/4 example without your ear settling on one of the two as the floor. It's hard.)

I'm not going to litigate this. The point worth keeping is that the African inheritance — the cross-rhythm tradition — does not actually work in the way Western theory wants to describe it. The bell pattern is not a decoration; it is the time. We're inheriting a vocabulary that was developed for music that operated differently, and it shows.

## Closing

The thing I wanted to say about *Live-Evil* — and what I think the record is finally trying to teach — is that the count is not the place to look. You can spend years trying to find the 7 or the 11 in a piece of electric Miles, and all you will find is that there isn't one. The bar is 4. The complication is on top of it.

This is the same answer hip-hop has been giving for forty years, in a different vocabulary. Dilla's nudge function and Henderson's funk vamp are not the same technique, but they are the same answer to the same question: *where does the rhythmic information actually live, if not in the bar line?*

Once you hear the question that way, a lot of music opens up.
